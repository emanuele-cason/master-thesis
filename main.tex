\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
High-Reynolds number turbulent flows dominate critical engineering applications, ranging from aircraft and other vehicles to wind turbines and turbomachinery. However, their simulation remains an open challenge: such flows are characterized by a multi-scale nature, requiring an enormous number of degrees of freedom. Wall-Modeled Large Eddy Simulation (WM-LES) addresses this problem by resolving the largest scales of the motion while modeling the smaller ones and the near-wall turbulent structures. In the present work, the Immersed Boundary Method (IBM) provides a flexible framework that avoids the complexity of body-fitted meshes. The presence of the immersed geometry is enforced in the discretized governing equations by a forcing algorithm coupled with the wall model.

The initial approach for the immersed boundary forcing in the solver was based on the identification of the interface through sign changes of the level set across adjacent cell nodes, resulting in the application of the forcing to a staircase-approximated wall. To improve the approach, a refined forcing technique was developed and integrated, based on the analytical computation of the cut-cell wetted area, coupled with a flux redistribution method. This geometric characterization redefines the numerical interface, allowing the wall-model forcing to be applied directly to the analytically evaluated surface of each control volume. The result is a more uniform and consistent boundary representation that introduces no significant computational overhead.

The proposed formulation was tested against two benchmarks: a canonical channel flow, demonstrating good agreement with the analytical Reichardt law, and the ERCOFTAC periodic hills, in which the solver successfully captured complex macroscopic flow topologies, including separation from a curved boundary, the formation of a free shear layer, and flow reattachment. To further assess the robustness of the numerical framework, a highly complex Airbus A320 wing-pylon-engine assembly was simulated under a inoperative engine configuration. The solver proved particularly stable under these demanding conditions, qualitatively reproducing several expected aerodynamic phenomena.

\chapter{Introduction}

% Large eddy simulation for incompressible flows - an introduction (2006, Springer) - P. Sagaut
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) is the study of fluids through a numerical computer-based approach, using algorithms to find solutions to the equations that describe the fluid behavior. The information produced has mainly two purposes: in the field of research, it allows a better understanding of the physical mechanisms governing the fluid motion, and the consequent modeling and control of such mechanisms. In the engineering analyses, fluid simulation is used as a prediction tool, especially during the product design phase, to assess flow characteristics and physical parameters, such as lift and drag forces generated, propagation of acoustic waves or convective heat transfer.

One fundamental aspect of fluid simulation regards the scale separation problem, in particular the spatial and temporal resolution of choice. To obtain a description of maximum quality, all the space-time scales of the solution must be taken into account while solving the unsteady Navier-Stokes equations. As will be discussed in Chapter \ref{sec:turbulent-flows}, it turns out that such a solution criterion is extremely restrictive for multi-scale phenomena, like in the case of turbulent flows, requiring an enormous amount of degrees of freedom.

To overcome the prohibitive computational costs associated with resolving all the space-time scales involved, multiple techniques with different levels of approximation can be employed, each one being best suited to specific applications and subject to its own limitations. Among the various techniques, overviewed in Chapter \ref{sec:simulation-techniques-overview}, Large Eddy Simulation (LES) represents a highly effective compromise between computational cost and physical accuracy. By resolving the large eddies and modeling only the smaller more universal scales, LES significantly reduces the computational cost compared to Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS). 

However, for wall-bounded flows at high Reynolds numbers, the resolution requirements for the near-wall turbulent structures in standard LES become once again computationally restrictive. To address the limitation, Wall-Modeled LES (WM-LES) can be adopted. By modeling this region of the flow rather than resolving it, WM-LES drastically reduces the required degrees of freedom, accepting a certain level of approximation in the near-wall dynamics. The mathematical framework governing the filtered Navier-Stokes equations and the specific sub-grid scales model and wall model closures employed in this work are detailed in Chapter \ref{sec:mathematical-framework}.

Another problematic aspect arises when complex geometries, such as industrial components, need to be simulated within the fluid domain. The traditional computational approach relies on the definition of body-fitted meshes. However, their generation is notoriously difficult, time-consuming, and computationally expensive, especially for intricate configurations. In this work, the Immersed Boundary Method (IBM) provides a flexible alternative to this issue by employing a simple background Cartesian grid in which the geometric model is numerically immersed. As explained in Chapter \ref{sec:numerical-framework}, the presence of the immersed body is enforced in the discretized governing equations through a localized forcing term. The evaluation of the forcing term in the original architecture of the solver relied on identifying the solid interface through sign changes of a level set function across adjacent cell nodes. This led to the application of the forcing term to a staircase-approximated wall. In this work, a refined method for the application of the forcing term, based on the analytical evaluation of the cut-cell wetted area and flux redistribution technique, was developed, integrated and tested.

To assess the accuracy and robustness of this new formulation, the solver was first tested against two benchmarks, presenting increasingly difficult flow topologies, as detailed in Chapter \ref{sec:test-cases}. These include a turbulent channel flow and the ERCOFTAC periodic hills, characterized by boundary layer separation and flow reattachment. Finally, to test the stability of the numerical framework to handle highly demanding geometric constraints, Chapter \ref{sec:complex-geometry-simulation} presents the application of the method to a representative industrial case, specifically an Airbus A320 wing-pylon-engine assembly under inoperative engine conditions.

\chapter{Turbulent flows}
\label{sec:turbulent-flows}

In most of the engineering applications, flows are prevalently turbulent. Generally this is the case of fluid processed by pumps, compressors or flows around cars, trains, airplanes and many more situations in the everyday surrounding.

Such flows are characterized by some specific properties. A key feature is the multi-scale nature of the vortical structures, whose dimensions span 6 to 8 orders of magnitude. Despite the deterministic nature (they can be described accurately by the Navier-Stokes equations), the strong dependence and sensitivity to initial conditions make their behavior chaotic. Furthermore, turbulent flows are intrinsically three-dimensional and unsteady, they show an high diffusivity (mixing of momentum, energy and species) and also an increased wall friction when compared to laminar flows.

The Navier-Stokes equations express the principles of mass and momentum conservation, governing the evolution of the flow field. In the current work, the fluid is assumed to be incompressible and Newtonian, with constant viscosity. Under these hypotheses, the governing equations can be formulated in their \textit{convective form} as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:NS-conv}
\begin{cases}
    \nabla \cdot \bm{u} = 0 \\[1.5ex]
    \dfrac{\partial \bm{u}}{\partial t} + \bm{u} \cdot \nabla \bm{u} = -\dfrac{\nabla p}{\rho} + \nabla \cdot (2\nu \bm{e}) + \bm{f}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
or recasting the system in its \textit{conservative form}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:NS-cons}
\begin{cases}
    \nabla \cdot \bm{u} = 0 \\[1.5ex]
    \dfrac{\partial \bm{u}}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\bm{u} \otimes \bm{u}) = -\dfrac{\nabla p}{\rho} + \nabla \cdot (2\nu \bm{e}) + \bm{f}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\bm{e}$ is the strain-rate tensor, and $\bm{f}$ represents the body forces.

The flow regime is described by the Reynolds number, a non-dimensional value representing the ratio between inertial and viscous forces, and defined as:
\begin{equation}
	Re = \frac{\rho \, U_0 \, L_0}{\mu} = \frac{U_0 \, L_0}{\nu}
\end{equation}
where $U_0$ and $L_0$ are the characteristic velocity and length scale of the flow. It is observed that, for higher values of $Re$, the magnitude of velocity gradients is progressively more intense, and the scale of smaller vortices decreases.

Considering the chaotic nature of the turbulence, a deterministic and instantaneous description of the phenomena is not always the most efficient option. Consequently, the analysis is typically focused on a statistical approach, considering averaged fields. It can be observed how those averaged fields resemble qualitatively the laminar behavior, and their dependence on the Reynolds number diminishes significantly.

\section{Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes equations}

To analytically formalize the idea of considering the average fields and the turbulent one separately, the Reynolds averaging operator and the Reynolds decomposition are here introduced. The former consists of an ensemble average over a number of realizations. In more practical terms this implies repeating the same experiment $N$ times, under identical conditions, and averaging the flow variables at the exact same location and time instant across all the independent measures.
Applying the Reynolds operator to the velocity component $u$ yields:
\begin{equation}
	\langle u(\bm{x}, t) \rangle = U(\bm{x}, t) = \frac{1}{N} \, \sum_{k=1}^{N \gg 1} u_{(k)} (\bm{x}, t)
\end{equation}
Conversely, the Reynolds decomposition is defined as:
\begin{equation}
	u(\bm{x}, t) = U(\bm{x}, t) + u'(\bm{x}, t)
\end{equation}
where $u'$ is the fluctuation component and $U$ is the averaged quantity.

Substituting the Reynolds decomposition into the Navier-Stokes equations \eqref{eq:NS-cons}, neglecting body forces for simplicity, and applying the Reynolds operator yields the Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) equations:
\begin{equation}
	\left\{
	\begin{aligned}
		 & \bm{\nabla} \cdot \bm{U} = 0                                                                                                           \\
		 & \frac{D\bm{U}}{Dt} = -\frac{\bm{\nabla} P}{\rho} + \bm{\nabla} \cdot (2\nu \bm{E}) - \bm{\nabla} \cdot \langle \bm{u}' \bm{u}' \rangle
	\end{aligned}
	\right.
	\label{eq:rans}
\end{equation}
where the term $\langle \bm{u}' \bm{u}' \rangle$ is the Reynolds stress tensor, and its modeling represents the closure problem of the RANS equations.

\section{Energy cascade}

The RANS equations introduced above, allow demonstrating some fundamental mechanisms governing the energy dynamics within turbulent flows. Omitting for brevity the detailed algebraic steps, the mean and turbulent flow kinetic energies can be defined respectively as:
\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{K} = \frac{1}{2} \, \bm{U} \cdot \bm{U} = \frac{1}{2} \, U_i \, U_i  \qquad \quad k = \frac{1}{2} \, \langle \bm{u}' \cdot \bm{u}' \rangle = \frac{1}{2} \, \langle u_i' \, u_i' \rangle
	\label{eq:kinetic-energy}
\end{equation}
By manipulating the momentum equation, it is possible to derive a formulation for both the time derivative of the mean kinetic energy and turbulent kinetic energy terms:
\begin{align}
	% Mean kinetic energy equation 
	 & \frac{D \mathcal{K}}{Dt} =
	% F_K
	\underbrace{ \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} \left[ \, -\frac{P}{\rho} \, U_j + 2\nu \, E_{ij} \, U_i - \langle u_i' u_j' \rangle \, U_i \, \right] \rule[-18pt]{0pt}{0pt} }_{\raisebox{-6pt}{$\bm{\nabla} \cdot \bm{F}_{\mathcal{K}}$}}
	% D_K
	\underbrace{ - 2\nu \, E_{ij} E_{ij} \vphantom{\frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} \left[ \frac{P}{\rho} \right]} \rule[-18pt]{0pt}{0pt} }_{\raisebox{-6pt}{$-\mathcal{D}$}}
	% P
	+ \underbrace{ \left( \langle u_i' u_j' \rangle \, \frac{\partial U_i}{\partial x_j} \right) \vphantom{\frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} \left[ \frac{P}{\rho} \right]} \rule[-18pt]{0pt}{0pt} }_{\raisebox{-6pt}{$-\mathcal{P}$}} \\[4ex]
	%
	% Turbulent kinetic energy equation
	 & \frac{D k}{D t} =
	% F_k
	\underbrace{ \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} \left[ \, -\frac{\langle p' u_j' \rangle}{\rho} + 2\nu \, \langle e_{ij}' u_i' \rangle - \frac{\langle u_i' u_i' u_j' \rangle}{2} \, \right] \vphantom{\frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} \left[ \frac{P}{\rho} \right]} \rule[-18pt]{0pt}{0pt} }_{\raisebox{-6pt}{$\bm{\nabla} \cdot \bm{F}_k$}}
	% Epsilon
	\underbrace{ - 2\nu \, \langle e_{ij}' e_{ij}' \rangle \vphantom{\frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} \left[ \frac{P}{\rho} \right]} \rule[-18pt]{0pt}{0pt} }_{\raisebox{-6pt}{$-\mathcal{E}$}}
	% P (Grouping)
	+ \underbrace{ \left( - \langle u_i' u_j' \rangle \, \frac{\partial U_i}{\partial x_j} \right) \vphantom{\frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} \left[ \frac{P}{\rho} \right]} \rule[-18pt]{0pt}{0pt} }_{\raisebox{-6pt}{$\mathcal{P}$}}
\end{align}
where $\bm{F}_{\mathcal{K}}$ and $\bm{F}_{k}$ are the fluxes of kinetic energy (spatial transport terms), $\mathcal{D}$ and $\mathcal{E}$ are the dissipation terms respectively for the mean and turbulent flows and $\mathcal{P}$ is the turbulent energy production. In a more compact form, the two equation can be written as:
\begin{equation}
	\frac{D \mathcal{K}}{Dt} = \bm{\nabla} \cdot \bm{F}_{\mathcal{K}} - \mathcal{D} - \mathcal{P}
	\qquad \quad
	\frac{D k}{Dt} = \bm{\nabla} \cdot \bm{F}_{k} - \mathcal{E} + \mathcal{P}
\end{equation}
Following the approach described by Pope \cite{turbulent-flows}, the equations can be integrated over a sufficiently large control volume $\Omega$, surrounding a free shear flow such as a turbulent jet ($Re \gg 1$), in stationary conditions. This perspective, and what result from the integration, allows to identify which component are the dominant in the energy dynamics.
\begin{equation}
	\int_{\Omega} \frac{\partial \mathcal{K}}{\partial t} \, d\Omega = \mathbb{C}_{\mathcal{K}} + \mathbb{F}_{\mathcal{K}} - \mathbb{D}_{\mathcal{K}} - \mathbb{P} \implies \mathbb{C}_{\mathcal{K}} \approx \mathbb{P}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
	\int_{\Omega} \frac{\partial k}{\partial t} \, d\Omega = \mathbb{C}_{k} + \mathbb{F}_{k} - \mathbbcal{E} + \mathbb{P} \implies \mathbb{P} \approx \mathbbcal{E}
\end{equation}
The analysis of the dominant terms in the energy balance equations reveals how the input of energy, associated with convection ($\mathbb{C}_{\mathcal{K}}$) and its viscous dissipation ($\mathbbcal{E}$) occur at different scales. The apparent decoupling between energy input and output is better described by Richardson's theory.

\section{Richardson's theory}
The first concept in Richardson’s view of the energy cascade is that the turbulence can be considered to be composed of eddies of different sizes \cite{turbulent-flows}.

Considering the eddies with the largest sizes, their characteristic dimensions can be evaluated as: $\ell_0 \approx L_0$ and $u_0 \approx u_{rms}' = \sqrt{(2/3) \cdot k} \approx (0.1 - 0.3) \, U_0$ (where the last relation comes from observation), thus their Reynolds number:
\begin{equation}
	Re_0 = \frac{u_0 \, \ell_0}{\nu} \approx \frac{U_0 \, L_0}{\nu} \gg 1
\end{equation}
this suggests how at these scales the direct effect of molecular viscosity is negligible.

Thus, in order to achieve dissipation, unstable large eddies transfer energy to smaller ones. This process continues until the scale $\ell = \eta$ is reached, at this level the associated Reynolds number becomes sufficiently small, or in other words the molecular viscosity begins to be effective in energy dissipation.

The energy transferred to smaller scales can be evaluated as:
\begin{equation}
	\frac{k_0}{\tau_0} \approx \frac{u_0^2}{\left( \ell_0 / u_0\right)} = \frac{u_0^3}{\ell_0} \propto \frac{U_0^3}{L_0} \propto \mathcal{E}
\end{equation}
the scaling of $\mathcal{E}$ with $U_0^3 / L_0$, independently from $\nu$ (at the high Reynolds numbers considered) proves to be consistent with the experimental results for free shear flows \cite{turbulent-flows}.

\section{Kolmogorov's theory}
With the Kolmogorov theory, a better characterization of the involved scales in high-Reynolds flows was proposed. The theory was stated in three hypotheses:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Local isotropy hypothesis:} \textit{At sufficiently high Reynolds numbers, the small-scales ($\ell \ll \ell_0$) turbulent motions are statistically isotropic.}

	      While larger eddies show an anisotropic behavior, as a result of flow geometry and boundary conditions, scales of motion under a certain length value (small-scales) have statistical properties that are independent of the large, anisotropic scales. Consequently, they exhibit a universal behavior, in the sense that they appear similar in every high-Reynolds number flow.

	\item \textbf{First similarity hypothesis:} \textit{In every turbulent flow at sufficiently high Reynolds number, the statistics of the small-scale motions have a universal form that is uniquely determined by $\nu$ and $\mathcal{E}$.}

	      As a consequence of this hypothesis, starting from the $\nu$ and $\mathcal{E}$ parameters, it is possible to identify a particular set of scales (Kolmogorov scales) with specific length, velocity and time characteristics. This result is obtained by dimensional analysis.
	      \begin{equation}
		      \eta = \left( \frac{\nu \, ^3}{\mathcal{E}} \right)^{1/4} \qquad
		      u_\eta = (\nu \mathcal{E})^{1/4} \qquad \,\,\,
		      \tau_\eta = \left( \frac{\nu}{\mathcal{E}} \right)^{1/2}
	      \end{equation}
	      These scales are considered the smallest active scales in the turbulent motion. As a support for this assertion it is possible to observe that the related Reynolds number is unity ($Re_\eta = \eta \cdot u_\eta / \nu = 1$), meaning that viscous forces effectively dominate over inertial forces, allowing the dissipation phenomena.

	      The extent of scale separation can be evaluated by considering the ratios of the smallest to largest scales. These ratios depend on the Reynolds number according to the following relations:
	      \begin{equation}
		      \frac{\ell_0}{\eta} \sim Re\,^{3/4} \, \qquad
		      \frac{u_0}{u_\eta} \sim Re\,^{1/4} \, \qquad
		      \frac{\tau_0}{\tau_\eta} \sim Re\,^{1/2}
		      \label{eq:k41-scale-ratios}
	      \end{equation}

	\item \textbf{Second similarity hypothesis:} \textit{In every turbulent flow at sufficiently high Reynolds number, the statistics of the motions of scale $\ell$ in the range $\eta \ll \ell \ll \ell_0$  have a universal form that is uniquely determined by $\mathcal{E}$, independent of $\nu$.}

	      This intermediate range of scales is known as the inertial sub-range. In this region, the eddies are large enough that viscous effects are negligible (since $\ell \gg \eta$), but small enough that the large-scale anisotropic geometry is not felt (since $\ell \ll \ell_0$). Consequently, the energy cascade is dominated mainly by inertial forces. Again by dimensional analysis:
	      \begin{equation}
		      u_{\ell} = (\mathcal{E} \, \ell \,)^{1/3} \qquad
		      \tau_{\ell} = \left( \frac{\ell \, ^2}{\mathcal{E}}  \,\right)^{1/3} \qquad
		      Re_{\ell} = \left( \frac{\ell}{\eta} \right)^{4/3}
		      \label{eq:k41-inertial-subrange}
	      \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

In summary, the energy input associated with the mean velocity field (for example as a result of convection) is in part transferred from one point of the domain to another by the flux term, another part is transferred down to the inertial subrange by the production term. This transfer to smaller and smaller scales continues until the Kolmogorov scales are reached, the effects of viscosity become dominant and the energy can be dissipated as heat.

\label{sec:wall-bounded-flows}

\subsection{Inertial momentum balance}
In the simple case of a uniform laminar flow above a smooth flat plate, the boundary layer develops in the flow direction. Its statistics vary primarily in the wall-normal $y$ direction and they are independent on the span-wise $z$ direction.

% Since the incompressible case is considered, the Bernoulli principle relate the mean free-stream pressure $P_e(x)$ with the mean velocity $U_e(x)$ so that:
% \begin{equation}
% 	P_e \, (x) + \frac{1}{2} \, \rho \, U_e(x)^2 = \textit{const} 
% 	\qquad \quad
% 	- \frac{d \, P_e}{dx} = \rho \, U_e \, \frac{d \, U_e}{dx}

At this point it is useful to recall the RANS equations \eqref{eq:rans}, developing their momentum $x$ and $y$ component, and neglecting the statistics variations in the $z$ direction:
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\partial U}{\partial t} + U \frac{\partial U}{\partial x} + V \frac{\partial U}{\partial y} =
	-\frac{1}{\rho} \frac{\partial P}{\partial x}
	+ \nu \left( \frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial y^2} \right)
	- \left( \frac{\partial \langle u'u' \rangle}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial \langle u'v' \rangle}{\partial y} \right)
	\label{eq:rans-wall-x}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + U \frac{\partial V}{\partial x} + V \frac{\partial V}{\partial y} =
	-\frac{1}{\rho} \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}
	+ \nu \left( \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial y^2} \right)
	- \left( \frac{\partial \langle u'v' \rangle}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial \langle v'v' \rangle}{\partial y} \right)
	\label{eq:rans-wall-y}
\end{equation}
By applying the classical order of magnitude analysis originally proposed by Prandtl~\cite{prandtl-dimensional-analysis}, based on the assumption that the boundary layer thickness is much smaller than the streamwise length scale, the equations above are further simplified. Moreover it is also possible to neglect the axial derivatives of the Reynolds stresses, that are small compared with lateral gradients \cite{turbulent-flows}. % See p. 148 Pope

For the cited assumptions, equation \eqref{eq:rans-wall-x} becomes:
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\partial U}{\partial t} + U \frac{\partial U}{\partial x} + V \frac{\partial U}{\partial y} =
	-\frac{1}{\rho} \frac{\partial P}{\partial x}
	+ \nu \frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial y^2}
	- \frac{\partial \langle u'v' \rangle}{\partial y}
	\label{eq:rans-tble}
\end{equation}
And can be rewritten as:
\begin{equation}
	\underbrace{
		\frac{\partial U}{\partial t} + U \frac{\partial U}{\partial x} + V \frac{\partial U}{\partial y}
		\vphantom{\frac{\partial}{\partial y} \left[ \frac{\partial U}{\partial y} \right]}
		\rule[-18pt]{0pt}{0pt}
	}_{\raisebox{-6pt}{\text{\footnotesize Inertial terms}}}
	=
	-\frac{1}{\rho} \frac{\partial P}{\partial x}
	+
	\frac{1}{\rho} \, \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \, \underbrace{ \left[ \mu \, \frac{\partial U}{\partial y} - \rho \langle u'v' \rangle \right]
		\rule[-18pt]{0pt}{0pt}
	}_{\raisebox{-6pt}{\text{\footnotesize Total stress $\tau(y)$}}}
	\label{eq:tble}
\end{equation}
The total shear stress $\tau(y)$ enclosed by brackets is composed by two contributions with different physical natures:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Viscous stress:} arises from the mean velocity gradients and is dominant in the immediate vicinity of the wall (inner region). It represents the molecular diffusion of momentum and is the only stress present in the laminar case.
	\item \textbf{Turbulent stress:} derives from the correlation of velocity fluctuations and represents the momentum transfer due to turbulent mixing. It prevails in the outer region, far from the wall, and vanishes in laminar conditions.
\end{itemize}

Under the same assumptions, equation \eqref{eq:rans-wall-y} can be simplified and integrated  as:
\begin{equation}
	0 =
	- \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}
	- \rho \, \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \langle v'^{\, 2} \rangle
	\quad
	\implies
	\quad
	0 =
	\frac{\partial}{\partial y} \left(
	P + \rho \, \langle v'^{\, 2} \rangle \right)
	\label{eq:pressure-tble}
\end{equation}
In the particular case of flow over a flat plate considered, since at the wall and in the far free-stream region the term $\langle v'^{\, 2} \rangle$ cancels out, it results that the mean wall pressure $P_w \, (x)$ equals the free-stream pressure $P_e \, (x)$.
\begin{equation}
	\left(
	P + \rho \, \langle v'^{\, 2} \rangle \right) = P_w \, (x) = P_e \, (x)
\end{equation}
Equation \eqref{eq:tble} represents the \textit{Inertial Momentum Balance}. The terms on the left-hand side account for the fluid inertia. These terms include both the unsteady acceleration and the spatial convection.
Their presence indicates a non-equilibrium state: the pressure gradient and the total shear stress do not simply balance each other, and the local velocity profile is not determined uniquely by the instantaneous force balance, but depends on the flow evolution.

\subsection{Equilibrium momentum balance}
\label{sec:equilibrium-momentum-balance}
The previous case can be further simplified considering the example of a fully developed channel flow, characterized by more restrictive assumptions. The geometry consists of two infinite flat plates placed at a distance $2h$, with the flow driven by a constant mean pressure gradient along the streamwise direction $x$. In this configuration, the statistics are assumed to be independent of the streamwise coordinate $x$ (except for the pressure), the spanwise coordinate $z$ and the time $t$:
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\partial}{\partial \, x} \, \langle ... \rangle = 0 \qquad \frac{\partial}{\partial \, z} \, \langle ... \rangle = 0 \qquad
	\frac{\partial}{\partial \, t} \, \langle ... \rangle = 0
\end{equation}
Due to the homogeneity in the $x$ direction, the mass balance equation simplifies to $\partial V / \partial y = 0$. Considering the impermeability condition at the wall ($V|_{y=0} = 0$), it follows that the wall-normal mean velocity component $V$ is null everywhere in the channel.
Consequently, in equation \eqref{eq:tble}, the inertial terms on the left-hand side vanish, and the $x$-momentum balance reduces to an equilibrium between the pressure forces and the gradient of the total stress:
\begin{equation}
	0 = -\frac{1}{\rho} \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{1}{\rho} \, \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \left[ \mu \frac{\partial U}{\partial y} - \rho \, \langle u'v' \rangle \right]
	\label{eq:eq-tble-1}
\end{equation}
Similarly to what was observed for equation \eqref{eq:pressure-tble}, the term $P + \rho \, \langle v'^{\, 2} \rangle$ is constant along $y$, the term $\langle v'^{\, 2} \rangle$ cancels out at the wall, and its derivative in $x$ is zero for the homogeneity assumptions. Consequently:
\begin{equation}
	\left(
	P + \rho \, \langle v'^{\, 2} \rangle \right) = P_w \, (x)
	\quad
	\implies
	\quad
	\frac{\partial}{\partial x} \left(
	P + \rho \, \langle v'^{\, 2} \rangle \right) = \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial P_w}{\partial x}
\end{equation}
And substituting the pressure gradient in equation \eqref{eq:eq-tble-1}:
\begin{equation}
	\frac{d P_w}{dx} = \frac{d}{dy} \left[ \mu \frac{dU}{dy} - \rho \langle u'v' \rangle \right] = \frac{d \tau}{dy} = \textit{const}
	\label{eq:eq-tble-2}
\end{equation}
While the pressure is a function of $x$, $\tau$ is a function of $y$ so, in order for the equation \eqref{eq:eq-tble-2} to be verified, their derivatives above must be equal to a constant value. This imply a linear behavior of the total shear stress along the $y$ direction, described by:
\begin{equation}
	\tau(y) = \tau_w \left( 1 - \frac{y}{h} \right)
	\quad \implies \quad
	\left\{ \, \,
	\begin{aligned}
		 & \tau(y=0)   = \tau_w  \\
		 & \tau(y=2h)  = -\tau_w
	\end{aligned}
	\right.
	\label{eq:linear-total-shear-stress}
\end{equation}
The data from DNS allows to evaluate the evolution of the two stress contributions, their plot over the half channel width is shown in Figure \ref{fig:channel-stresses}.
\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{img/channel-stresses.jpg}
	\caption{Contributions of normalized shear stress along half channel width, evaluated from DNS data \cite{turbulent-flows} \cite{channel-stresses}. Dashed (solid) lines indicate lower (higher) Reynolds numbers.}
	\label{fig:channel-stresses}
\end{figure}

Observing the data of the contributions, it is possible to identify two regions, and define for each one some important parameters:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Inner layer $(y/h \ll 1)$:} near the wall with a prevalence of the viscous effects. At increasing Reynolds number this region becomes progressively thinner. Its characteristic wall friction velocity and viscous length scale are defined as:
	      \begin{equation}
		      \makebox[5.0cm][c]{$\displaystyle u_\tau = \sqrt{\frac{\tau_w}{\rho}}$}
		      \makebox[5.0cm][c]{$\displaystyle \delta_{\nu} = \frac{\nu}{u_\tau}$}
	      \end{equation}
	      Using these scales, the wall-normal coordinate and the mean velocity can be expressed in inner variables as:
	      \begin{equation}
		      \makebox[5.0cm][c]{$\displaystyle U^+ = \frac{U}{u_\tau}$}
		      \makebox[5.0cm][c]{$\displaystyle y^+ = \frac{y}{\delta_\nu} = \frac{y \, u_\tau}{\nu}$}
	      \end{equation}
	      It can be noticed that, to assume $(y/h \ll 1)$ imply $\tau(y) \approx \tau_w$ and thus $d P_w / d x = d \tau / dy \approx 0$ (see equations \eqref{eq:linear-total-shear-stress} and \eqref{eq:eq-tble-2}). This means that the gradients involved cannot be excessive in order for the formulation to be valid.
	\item \textbf{Outer layer $(y/\delta_{\nu} \gg 1)$:} where the turbulent stress component is dominant. The characteristic velocity and length scales are:
	      \begin{equation}
		      \makebox[5.0cm][c]{$\displaystyle u_\tau = \sqrt{-\frac{h}{\rho}\, \frac{\partial P}{\partial x}}$}
		      \makebox[5.0cm][c]{{\Large $h$}}
	      \end{equation}
\end{itemize}
The ratio between the two characteristic length scales can be defined as the friction Reynolds number:
\begin{equation}
	\frac{h}{\delta_{\nu}} = \frac{u_{\tau} \, h}{\nu} = Re_{\tau}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Wall laws}
\label{sec:wall-laws}
Despite the previous separate definition of the two regions, the mean behavior of the flow varies with continuity moving away from the wall and so do the mathematical laws that describe the velocity profile:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Viscous sub-layer:} Inside the inner layer, in the close proximity of the wall, the flow is dominated by viscous effects. The linear relation is considered well verified:
	      \begin{equation}
		      \makebox[5.0cm][c]{$\displaystyle U^+ = y^+$}
		      \makebox[5.0cm][c]{$\displaystyle y^+ < 5$}
	      \end{equation}
	\item \textbf{Buffer layer:} In the transition region ($5 < y^+ < 30$), neither the linear or the logarithmic law provides an accurate description, as viscous and turbulent stresses are of comparable magnitude. Consequently, the velocity profile is described by semi-empirical functions (such as Van Driest damping or Spalding's law) that smoothly connect the viscous sub-layer to the logarithmic region.
	\item \textbf{Log-law region:} Moving towards the outer layer, in the region ($30 < y^+, \text{ and } y/h < 0.3$), a common behavior arises from the overlap between the inner and outer scalings. In the inner layer	the mean flow is described by the logarithmic law, still dependent on: the $y^+$ coordinate; the Von Karman constant ($\kappa = 0.41$); the integration constant ($B \approx 5.2$).
	      \begin{equation}
		      \makebox[5.0cm][c]{$\displaystyle U^+ = \frac{1}{\kappa} \, \text{ln} \, y^+ + B$}
		      \makebox[5.0cm][c]{$\displaystyle y^+ > 30$}
	      \end{equation}
	      In the outer layer, the flow is described by a velocity defect with respect to the centerline velocity $U_C$. The defect law is dependent on: the $y/h$ normalized coordinate; the Von Karman constant ($\kappa = 0.41$); the integration constant $B_1$, specific for the type of flow. Such defect law in the overlap zone also assumes a logarithmic formulation:
	      \begin{equation}
		      \makebox[5.0cm][c]{$\displaystyle \frac{U_C - U}{u_\tau} = - \, \frac{1}{\kappa} \, \text{ln} \, \frac{y}{h} + B_1$}
		      \makebox[5.0cm][c]{$\displaystyle y/h < 0.3$}
	      \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Simulation techniques overview}
\label{sec:simulation-techniques-overview}
The theoretical framework just introduced, highlights the wide range of scales of motion involved in the turbulent dynamics. In order to describe properly the fluid flow, it is necessary to take into account in the simulation all the space-time scales affecting the phenomena. This can result in an enormous amount of degrees of freedom, especially for high-Reynolds flows.

\section{Direct Numerical Simulation}
The most direct approach to perform a simulation, is choosing a space (grid) and time discretization able to represent all the space-time scales, included the smaller ones (Kolmogorov scales), in a non-stationary, three dimensional domain.

In terms of accuracy and level of description provided it is unrivalled at the point that these numerical experiments can be considered directly analogous to actual laboratory experiments \cite{cfd-anderson} and does not need validation. However for the number of degrees of freedom involved its cost is extremely high and its applicability is limited to low or moderate Reynolds numbers \cite{turbulent-flows}.

The scale separation problem can be illustrated assuming to discretize a domain with dimensions $L \sim \ell_0$. To properly resolve the dissipative structures, the grid spacing $\Delta$ must be comparable to the Kolmogorov scale $\eta$. Applying the relations~\eqref{eq:k41-scale-ratios}, the number of grid points required along each axis is on the order of $Re\,^{3/4}$ and in terms of time discretization, the required number of time-steps for a $\tau_0$ interval would be on the order of $Re\,^{1/2}$. Overall, the computational cost of the simulation scales with the Reynolds number, following a $Re\,^{11/4} \approx Re\,^3$ relation.

As a consequence, this type of simulation for large Reynolds number, such as those related to application in the aeronautical field ($Re \sim 10^8$) requires computer resources very much greater than currently available super computer capacities, and is therefore not practicable \cite{les-sagaut}.

\section{RANS-based simulation}
\label{sec:rans}

% How it works 
An approach capable of reducing heavily the number of degrees of freedom to be computed is based on the Reynolds-Averaged Navier Stokes equations \eqref{eq:rans} previously described. In contrast to the direct resolution of all turbulent scales, such method focuses on the determination of the mean flow field. The contribution related to the fluctuation field (transfer of momentum due to turbulent fluctuations) appears in the equations as the Reynolds stress tensor $\langle \bm{u}' \bm{u}' \rangle$ that needs to be determined to close the system of equations.

The structure of the Reynolds stress tensor is:
\begin{equation}
	\langle \bm{u}' \bm{u}' \rangle =
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\langle u'^2 \rangle & \langle u'v' \rangle & \langle u'w' \rangle \\
		\langle v'u' \rangle & \langle v'^2 \rangle & \langle v'w' \rangle \\
		\langle w'u' \rangle & \langle w'v' \rangle & \langle w'^2 \rangle
	\end{bmatrix}
	\label{eq:reynolds-stress-tensor}
\end{equation}
it is constituted by nine components, but considering its symmetry, the independent terms are six. Recalling equation \eqref{eq:kinetic-energy} that defines the turbulent kinetic energy, it is possible to observe that the trace of the tensor corresponds to twice the turbulent kinetic energy $k$.

% Advantages
From a computational perspective, the grid requirements for RANS simulations are significantly less stringent than for DNS. The mesh must only be fine enough to resolve the gradients of the mean flow and the geometry, rather than the smallest Kolmogorov scales. Consequently, the computational cost does not scale theoretically with the Reynolds number as strictly as in DNS, making RANS the industry standard for high-Reynolds number engineering applications \cite{cfd-blazek}, with best performances for attached flows.

% Disadvantages
However, this reduction in computational effort comes at the cost of physical fidelity. By modeling the entire spectrum of turbulence, RANS simulations rely heavily on empirical constants and assumptions (e.g., isotropy of turbulence) that are not always accurate in complex flow regimes. Another fundamental limitation arises from the time-averaging procedure itself, that filters the entire dynamic content of the flow. Consequently, they are unable to capture transient phenomena, which are critical in many aerodynamic applications. While the mean flow representation is often sufficient for preliminary design, it fails when the physical unsteadiness of the large scales is dominant. To address cases where global flow instabilities must be resolved, the Unsteady RANS (URANS) approach, reviewed in a later section, can be adopted.

% Pro vs cons largely depend on the model
While those characteristics are generally true, it should be noted that the advantages and limitations of RANS simulations are significantly dependent on the turbulence model of choice. To have an overall understanding of their range of applicability, the main properties of those models will be summarized below.

To resolve the closure problem, varying levels of approximation can be adopted. Those approaches can be classified in the first place based on the order of resolved and modeled components (statistical moments).

\subsection{First-order closures}
\label{sec:first-order-closures}
% How it works
The most common and simple class of approaches is represented by first-order closures. In this case the Reynolds stress tensor (that is a second moment), is determined through a relation with the mean flow velocity gradients (first moment). First-order closures can be further classified in linear, mostly based on the Boussinesq hypothesis, and non-linear eddy-viscosity formulations.

% Linear models <-> Bousinnesq hp
In linear formulations, the modeling of the Reynolds stress tensor can be split in two contributions: a isotropic one, related to $k$, and a deviatoric one. To model the latter, the Boussinesq hypothesis is introduced. This assumption relies on a direct analogy with a laminar velocity field, assuming that the turbulent shear stresses are linearly proportional to the mean strain rate tensor $E_{ij}$ through a scalar coefficient known as the eddy viscosity, $\nu_T$:
\begin{equation}
	-\langle \, u_i' \, u_j' \, \rangle = - \frac{2}{3} \, k \, \delta_{ij} + 2 \, \nu_T \, E_{ij}
	\label{eq:boussinesq-model}
\end{equation}
The fundamental advantage of this hypothesis is the significant reduction in the problem complexity. It transforms the task of computing six independent tensor components into the determination of a single scalar field, $\nu_T$, that represent a function of the fluid local state of motion. However this approach introduces significant limitations. The assumption of local equilibrium between the mean strain tensor $E_{ij}$ and the deviatoric part of the turbulent stress tensor and its consequent independence on fluid rigid rotations is often excessively restrictive for flows characterized by massive separation, strong curvature, or unsteady large-scale structures \cite{cfd-blazek} \cite{tubulence-wilcox}.

% Non-linear models
With the development of more complex non-linear eddy viscosity models, the limitations discussed above are in part mitigated. They extend the relation between the Reynolds stress tensor and $E_{ij}$, involving the mean flow rotation tensor $\Omega_{ij}$ and including also non-linear quadratic and cubic terms \cite{rans-nl-model-lumley}.

% Number of governing equations
First-order turbulence closures can be also classified by the number of governing equations used, those with a greater number of equation usually provide a fuller description of turbulence.
\begin{itemize}
	\item Algebraic or zero-equations models such as the uniform turbulent viscosity one are characterized by extremely limited ranges of applicability.
	\item One-equation models are also very limited in possible application fields, an example is the Spalart-Allmaras model, designed for aerodynamic flows. The only real advantages of using one-equation model rather than a two-equation model are related with stability and simplicity of the numerical solution \cite{tubulence-wilcox}.
	\item Two-equation models such as $k-\mathcal{E}$, $k-\omega$ and their blended implementations like Shear Stress Transfer (SST) are the most widely employed and for their importance will be further analyzed below.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Model \boldmath$k - \mathcal{E}$} The $k - \mathcal{E}$ model is based on the evaluation of the turbulent kinetic energy and dissipation rate. One of the main limitations is related to near-wall behavior \cite{cfd-blazek}.
\begin{itemize}
	\item In low Reynolds number turbulence models there is the need of some damping functions to limit $k$ and $\mathcal{E}$ and an high grid resolution (first node at $y+ \leq 1$) in order to stay valid and resolve the viscous sub-layer.
	\item By contrast, high Reynolds number turbulence models don't need damping functions, they are less stringent in terms of grid resolution (first node inside $10 \leq y+ \leq 100$) but require the implementation of wall functions, often based in the logarithmic wall-law, with the consequent loss of accuracy in case of a separated flow.
\end{itemize}
Since the method is based on the Boussinesq assumption, it inherits all the limitations related to the linear eddy viscosity modeling already discussed previously.
A third aspect regards the (non) universality: the model requires \textit{ad hoc} adjustments of its calibration constants based on the nature of the considered flow \cite{turbulent-flows}. It also degrade in performance with adverse pressure gradients.

\subsubsection{Model \boldmath$k - \omega$} The $k - \omega$ model is the second most widely used two-equation model. The term $\omega$ is simply defined as: $\omega = \mathcal{E}/k$. It better performs for boundary layer flows and in presence of moderate adverse pressure gradients. The main drawback instead, is related to its sensibility to the arbitrary choice of $\omega$ boundary condition value \cite{turbulent-flows} \cite{tubulence-wilcox}.

\subsubsection{Model SST} The Shear Stress Transport (SST) model merges the $k-\omega$ formulation with a high Reynolds number $k-\mathcal{E}$ model, with the intent of combining the advantages of the two. The $k-\omega$ is used in the boundary layer since it doesn't require damping functions and it better performs in presence of adverse pressure gradients, instead $k-\mathcal{E}$ is employed in wake regions of the boundary layer. The switching between the models is ensured by a blending function, dependent on wall proximity.

\subsection{Second-order closures}
Second-order closures, unlike first-order models that rely on the Boussinesq hypothesis represent a more complex and flexible class of approaches to determine the Reynolds stress tensor. Reynolds Stress Transport (RST) models allow to solve individual transport equations for the six independent component of the Reynolds stress tensor. This approach naturally include effects of streamline curvature, sudden changes in strain rate, secondary motions and the history effects \cite{tubulence-wilcox}. With a similar accuracy, but a slightly lower level of description and complexity, the Algebraic Reynolds Stress (ARS) models employ only two transport equations, generally for the turbulent kinetic energy and for the dissipation rate. Despite their increased accuracy both RST and ARS models suffer numerical issues and they are more computationally demanding, so first-order models are more widely used in practice \cite{cfd-blazek}.

\subsection{Unsteady RANS (URANS)}
While in the stationary case the mean value can be considered as a time average, in the non-stationary case the ensemble average is used, more similarly to the theoretical description. Also, in such cases, the approach is to apply a RANS model in order to resolve some of the unsteady dynamics of the flow.

In the case of unsteady flows, this method can be substantially more successful in determining the mean flow than a steady RANS computation. By contrast, for the model assumption to be valid, a clear separation of scales is necessary, condition that is not always verified, so it is useful to distinguish between two cases. When unsteadiness is triggered by boundary conditions, scale separation generally holds and modeling assumptions remain valid. Instead, in presence of internal instabilities (e.g., bluff body flows), scale separation usually does not hold. Consequently, especially in this case the confidence in the result is limited without experimental validation \cite{urans-hybrid}.

\section{Large Eddy Simulation}
% Introduction
The Large Eddy Simulation (LES) represents an intermediate approach between the high-fidelity of DNS and the low computational cost of RANS. The idea behind this technique is based on the observation that turbulent flows contain a wide range of length and time scales, which play different roles in the flow dynamics.

% Overview
The large-scale structures, often referred to as "eddies", contain the majority of the turbulent kinetic energy and are responsible for the transport of momentum and conserved properties \cite{cfd-blazek}. These scales are strictly dependent on the boundary conditions and the flow geometry, making them highly anisotropic and difficult to model. Conversely, the small-scale structures tend to be more isotropic and universal in their behavior, essentially acting as a sink for the kinetic energy transferred from the larger scales (energy cascade). Based on this distinction, LES applies a spatial filtering operation to the Navier-Stokes equations. The large, energy-carrying motions are explicitly resolved on the computational grid, while the effects of the unresolved scales, those smaller than the grid cut-off width, are modeled using Subgrid-Scale (SGS) models.

% Advantages
This strategy offers significant advantages over RANS approaches. Since the large-scale unsteady motions are resolved directly rather than averaged, LES is capable of capturing transient phenomena and flow instabilities, which are critical in aerodynamic flows involving separation or bluff bodies. The arbitrary aspects are confined only to the small scales, making the simulation less dependent on model calibration compared to RANS, where the entire turbulent spectrum is modeled.

% Disadvantages
However, these advantages come with a substantial computational cost. In terms of grid resolution requirements, LES places itself in an intermediate position between DNS and RANS, only requiring the resolution of the structures down to the cut-off width $\Delta$. Consequently, the number of degrees of freedom is significantly lower than that of DNS, and substantially higher than RANS, where the mesh is only required to resolve the mean flow gradients. This requirement becomes particularly critical in wall-bounded flows, where the physical size of the energy-carrying eddies decreases proportionally to the distance from the wall \cite{turbulent-flows}. In the so-called Wall-Resolved LES (WR-LES), the need to resolve these inner boundary layer structures leads to a computational cost that scales significantly with the Reynolds number ($Re^{13/7}$) \cite{wrles-re-chapman} \cite{wrles-re-choi2012}, with possible prohibitive costs. To overcome this limitation and apply LES to high-Reynolds number flows of engineering interest, Wall-Modeled LES (WM-LES) approaches are adopted to bypass the strict resolution requirements of the near-wall region.

% Postpone discussion
Considering the central role of this methodology in the present work, and specifically the application of Wall-Modeled LES, this topic will be addressed in detail in the following chapter.

\section{Hybrid RANS/LES Simulation}
% Basic idea = main advantage
Another possible approach to drastically reduce the computational cost of simulating turbulent flows is to is to couple LES with the RANS models. The fundamental idea behind this strategy is to perform LES only in those regions where it is strictly necessary, typically where the flow is dominated by large-scale structures or massive separation. Conversely, the RANS approach is employed in the remainder of the domain, such as in the boundary layer regions, where it is known to be reliable and computationally efficient.

% Classification
Following the classification proposed by Fröhlich and von Terzi \cite{urans-hybrid}, these techniques are categorized into \textit{unified} and \textit{segregated} modeling.

% Unified = blending (pro/con) + interfacing (pro/con)
Unified modeling utilizes the same velocity transport equation in the entire domain, while the transition between RANS and LES models is managed locally. The switching between different models can be performed with a \textit{blending} function approach or by using a pure LES model in one part of the domain and a pure RANS model in the other, with the so called \textit{interfacing} approach, this is the case for instance of Detached Eddy Simulations (DES). While this approach is user-friendly as it avoids explicit zonal boundaries, it suffers from the potential existence of a "gray area" at the interface.

% Segregated (pro/con)
Segregated modeling instead require the \textit{a priori} decomposition of the domain into distinct regions. In each sub-domain an almost stand-alone RANS or LES computation are performed and they are coupled via boundary conditions at the interfaces. This allow each model to operate in its regime of validity, where it better performs. However, this method requires prior knowledge of the flow physics to define sub-domains and involves complex implementation for the coupling across interfaces.

\chapter{Mathematical framework}
\label{sec:mathematical-framework}
The review of simulation techniques presented in the previous chapter highlighted the capabilities and limitations of different turbulence modeling strategies. In the present work, the Large Eddy Simulation (LES) approach is selected to effectively capture the unsteady dynamics of the flow. This chapter details the mathematical framework of the solver. As introduced previously, the LES approach relies on the application of a spatial filtering operation to the Navier-Stokes equations. This represents the starting point for the derivation of the governing equations, where the large scales are explicitly resolved, and the effects of the unresolved scales are modeled using Subgrid-Scale (SGS) and wall models.

\section{Filtering operation}
Filtering is the operation that allows to mathematically separate the turbulent scales of motion. This decomposition is determined by a characteristic arbitrary cutoff width $\Delta_F$. In the spectral domain, this procedure acts as a low-pass filter: it retains the low-frequency, large-scale contributions, while the high-frequency fluctuations associated with spatial scales smaller than $\Delta_F$ are cut off to be modeled. Considering a generic field, this decomposition can be mathematically represented as:
\begin{equation}
	\phi \, (\bm{x}, \, t) = \widetilde{\phi} \, (\bm{x}, \, t) + \phi'' \, (\bm{x}, \, t)
\end{equation}
Considering a homogeneous and isotropic case, where the filter acts independently of position and orientation, the general space-time filtering operation can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
	\widetilde{\phi} \, (\bm{x}, \, t) = \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} \! \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} \phi \, (\bm{r}, \, \tau) \; G \, (\bm{x} - \bm{r}, \, t - \tau) \, d\tau \, d^3 \, \bm{r} = G \ast \phi
	\label{eq:les-filter-operation}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $\phi$ represents the generic flow variable.
	\item $\widetilde{\phi}$ is the filtered generic flow variable.
	\item $\phi''$ is the residual/sub-grid component.
	\item The double integral represent a temporal (one dimensional) and spatial (three dimensional) "scan" of the entire domain.
	\item $G$ is the filter kernel, dependent on the choice of $\Delta_F$.
\end{itemize}
Formally, this expression represents a convolution product between the physical quantity and the filter function. In simpler terms, this is a sort of weighted average operation of the values assumed by the field in the proximity of the considered point.

It should be noted that, despite the equation \eqref{eq:les-filter-operation} represents a filtering operation in the general case (for both space and time), the imposition of a spatial filtering automatically induces an implicit time filtering, according to the Kolmogorov's relations \eqref{eq:k41-inertial-subrange}, so it is possible to consider sufficient the spatial filtering alone \cite{les-sagaut}.

\subsection{Properties of the filtering operator}

In order to derive the equations for LES from Navier-Stokes equations, the filtering operator is required to satisfy three fundamental properties:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Conservation of constants:} The filter kernel must be normalized, such that the integral over the entire domain equals unity. This ensures that the filtering of a constant value returns the constant itself, preserving the global conservation of physical quantities.
	      \begin{equation}
		      \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} \! \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} G(\bm{x}, \tau) \, d\tau \, d^3\bm{x} = 1
	      \end{equation}

	\item \textbf{Linearity:} The filtering operation is linear, meaning that the filter of a sum is equal to the sum of the filtered quantities.
	      \begin{equation}
		      \widetilde{\phi + \psi} = \widetilde{\phi} + \widetilde{\psi}
	      \end{equation}

	\item \textbf{Commutation with differentiation:} The result is independent of the sequence in which differentiation and filtering are performed.
	      \begin{equation}
		      \frac{\partial \widetilde{\phi}}{\partial s} = \widetilde{\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial s}}, \qquad s = \bm{x}, t
	      \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

It is important to observe that a filter satisfying these properties differs substantially from the Reynolds averaging operator used in RANS. As a consequence:
\begin{equation}
	\widetilde{\widetilde{\phi}} \neq \widetilde{\phi} \qquad \widetilde{\phi''} \neq 0
\end{equation}
This implies that applying the filter twice produces a result different from a single application and the filtered fluctuation is not necessarily zero.

\subsection{Explicit and implicit filtering}
\label{sec:filtering}

It is important to distinguish between explicit and implicit filtering.

The explicit filtering approach involves the application of a distinct mathematical operator to the flow field, with a defined characteristic length $\Delta_F$, independent on the grid resolution $\Delta$. The three most common explicit filters are (Figure \ref{fig:les-filters}):
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Box or top-hat filter:} performs a local spatial average of the flow variables over a defined control volume. Its oscillating transfer function causes the introduction of high wavenumber noise.

	\item \textbf{Gaussian filter:} is based on a spatial Gaussian distribution. It has smooth characteristics in both physical and spectral spaces preventing the generation of numerical oscillations.

	\item \textbf{Sharp spectral cutoff filter:} eliminates all Fourier modes exceeding a specific cutoff wavenumber. Although it provides a definitive separation of scales, its non-locality in physical space can induce significant oscillations.
\end{itemize}

This explicit approach allows decouple the filter length and the grid resolution obtaining a better control on which scales are resolved and which are modeled. This has important implications in terms of stability control and allows to perform mesh convergence analysis varying the grid resolution alone.

Conversely, the implicit filtering approach relies on the computational grid and the discretization scheme to act as the filter. In this case, the filter width is coupled with the local mesh resolution ($\Delta_F = \Delta$), assuming that the grid itself defines the cutoff frequency without the need for an external mathematical operator. This allows to save a relevant part of the computational cost.

\section{Governing equations}

Applying the filtering operation to the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations, the governing equations for LES are obtained:
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:les-equations}
	\left\{
	\hspace{0.15cm}
	\begin{aligned}
		 & \frac{\partial \, \widetilde{u}_i}{\partial \, x_i} = 0                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              \\[12pt]
		 & \frac{\partial \, \widetilde{u}_i}{\partial \, t} + \widetilde{u}_j \, \frac{\partial \, \widetilde{u}_i}{\partial \, x_j} = - \, \frac{1}{\rho} \, \frac{\partial \, \widetilde{p}}{\partial \, x_i} + \frac{\partial}{\partial \, x_j} \left( 2 \, \nu \, \widetilde{e}_{ij} \right) - \frac{\partial}{\partial \, x_j} \left( \widetilde{u_i \, u_j} - \widetilde{u}_i \, \widetilde{u}_j \right) + f_i
	\end{aligned}
	\right.
\end{equation}
where $\widetilde{e}_{ij}$ represents the resolved rate-of-strain tensor and $\tau_{ij}^R$ is the residual (or sub-grid-scale) stress tensor:
\begin{equation}
	\widetilde{e}_{ij} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{\partial \, \widetilde{u}_i}{\partial \, x_j} + \frac{\partial \, \widetilde{u}_j}{\partial \, x_i} \right)
	\qquad
	\tau_{ij}^R = \left( \widetilde{u_i u_j} - \widetilde{u}_i \, \widetilde{u}_j \right)
\end{equation}
The residual stress tensor $\tau_{ij}^R$ represents the effect of the unresolved scales on the resolved field and, being symmetric ($\tau_{ij}^R = \tau_{ji}^R \,$), it introduces six independent components.

The system consists of only 4 equations (1 continuity and 3 momentum equations), while the dependent variables to be determined are 10: the three filtered velocity components $\widetilde{u}_i$, the filtered pressure $\widetilde{p}$, and the six independent components of the residual stress tensor. Since the number of unknowns (10) exceeds the number of available equations (4), the system is mathematically unclosed. Therefore, a closure model is required to express the unknown stresses $\tau_{ij}^R$ in terms of the resolved flow variables.

\subsection{Sub-Grid Scales modeling}
The modeling of the coupling term between resolved and sub-grid scales has to be performed on the basis of the resolved scales alone. As explained by Sagaut \cite{les-sagaut} this can be done with two approaches:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Structural modeling:} Allows for the modeling of the entire tensor $\tau_{ij}^R$, from the field $\widetilde{\bm{u}}$, requiring a relevant knowledge of the sub-grid scales structures. This is feasible when small scales shows an universal structure or a clear correlation with the structure of the resolved field.
	\item \textbf{Functional modeling:} Consist in the modeling of the action of the tensor $\tau_{ij}^R$, without determining the tensor itself. This require an understanding of the nature of the interaction and an effect of the small scales on the large universal in character.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Smagorinsky model}
As most of the strategies for functional modeling, the Smagorinsky approach models the sub-grid scale tensor $\tau_{ij}^R$, splitting it in a isotropic and a deviatoric contribution:
\begin{equation}
	- \, \tau_{ij}^R = - \, \frac{2}{3} \, k_R \, \delta_{ij} + 2 \, \nu_R \, \widetilde{e}_{ij}
\end{equation}
The first term is related to the sub-grid kinetic energy $k_R$, while the second one is modeled as a term linearly proportional to the deformation tensor of the resolved field $\widetilde{e}_{ij}$ through the sub-grid scale eddy viscosity coefficient $\nu_R$.

Substituting this closure into the filtered momentum equation \eqref{eq:les-equations}, the isotropic part of the residual stress is absorbed into the pressure term. Defining a modified pressure $\widetilde{p}$ (which now includes the trace of the sub-grid stress tensor) and grouping the molecular and eddy viscosities, the governing equation for the large scales becomes:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \, \widetilde{u}_i}{\partial \, t} + \widetilde{u}_j \, \frac{\partial \, \widetilde{u}_i}{\partial \, x_j} = - \, \frac{1}{\rho} \, \frac{\partial \, \widetilde{p}}{\partial \, x_i} + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} \left[ (\nu + \nu_{R}) \left( \frac{\partial \widetilde{u}_i}{\partial x_j} + \frac{\partial \widetilde{u}_j}{\partial x_i} \right) \right] + f_i
\end{equation}
Exploiting the incompressibility constraint ($\nabla \cdot \bm{\widetilde{u}} = 0$) to simplify the viscous term, it is possible to retrieve the formulation implemented in the numerical solver. The equation can be rewritten in vector notation as:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \, \bm{\widetilde{u}}}{\partial t} + \bm{\widetilde{u}} \cdot \nabla \bm{\widetilde{u}} = - \frac{1}{\rho} \, \nabla \widetilde{p} + \nabla \cdot \left[ (\nu + \nu_{R}) \, \left(\nabla \bm{\widetilde{u}} + \left( \nabla \bm{\widetilde{u}} \, \right)^T \right) \right] + \bm{f}
	\label{eq:les-mom-equation}
\end{equation}
In the Smagorinsky model the $\nu_R$ scalar is defined as:
\begin{equation}
	\nu_R = (C_S \, \Delta_F)^2 \, |\widetilde{e}_{ij}| \qquad \quad |\widetilde{e}_{ij}| := \sqrt{2 \, \widetilde{e}_{ij} \, \widetilde{e}_{ij}}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta_F$ is the sub-grid characteristic length scale (or filter size), $|\widetilde{e}_{ij}|$ is a characteristic sub-grid velocity (defined above) and $C_S$ is a tuning constant.

The idea behind the definition of the eddy viscosity $\nu_R$ is to emulate the energy transfer between resolved scales and sub-grid ones, by introducing an artificial dissipation at the $\Delta_F$ scale.

\paragraph{Model limitations}

While the model satisfies some useful properties, like its independence on coordinate translation and rotation and its easy implementation on any kind of computational grid, its predictions suffer from several aspects:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Insensitivity to rotational flow structures:} To understand the main limitations of the Smagorinsky model, it is useful to recall the formulation of the velocity gradient tensor $\nabla \, \widetilde{\bm{u}}$:
	      \begin{equation}
		      \nabla \, \widetilde{\mathbf{u}} = \frac{\partial \, \widetilde{u}_i}{\partial \, x_j} =
		      \underbrace{ \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{\partial \, \widetilde{u}_i}{\partial \, x_j} + \frac{\partial \, \widetilde{u}_j}{\partial \,x_i} \right) \rule[-15pt]{0pt}{0pt} }_{\text{\footnotesize $\widetilde{e}_{ij}$ (Strain rate)}}
		      +
		      \underbrace{ \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{\partial \, \widetilde{u}_i}{\partial \, x_j} - \frac{\partial \, \widetilde{u}_j}{\partial \,x_i} \right) \rule[-15pt]{0pt}{0pt} }_{\text{\footnotesize $\widetilde{\omega}_{ij}$ (Rotation rate)}}
	      \end{equation}
	      The studies of Wray and Hunt \cite{wray-hunt} of the properties of isotropic turbulence structures, based on DNS analyses, showed that while kinetic energy is concentrated in the streams, the dissipation phenomenon occurs in:
	      \begin{itemize}
		      \item Eddies: where vorticity is dominant over irrotational strain ($|\bm{\widetilde{\omega}}| \gg |\bm{\widetilde{e}}|$).
		      \item Convergence zones: where the prevalent term is the irrotational strain ($|\bm{\widetilde{e}}| \gg |\bm{\widetilde{\omega}}|$).
	      \end{itemize}
	      Since the Smagorinsky formulation takes into account the $\widetilde{e_{ij}}$ term alone, it is sensitive only to convergence zones, and it fails to correctly model regions where vorticity is dominant.

	\item \textbf{Inadequacy for pure shear and transitional flows: } In the case of pure shear flows, the large values of the $|\widetilde{e}_{ij}|$ invariant causes an overestimation of the energy to be virtually dissipated, preventing the growth of instabilities and consequently failing to reproduce the laminar-to-turbulent transition.

	\item \textbf{Incorrect near-wall behavior:} By construction, the Smagorinsky formulation yields a non-zero eddy viscosity $\nu_t$ whenever a velocity gradient exists. However, turbulent fluctuations are naturally damped near solid boundaries, implying that $\nu_t$ should disappear at the wall. A common approach to correct this behavior is the use of Van Driest damping functions. While this approach can improve the results, this is an ad hoc modification that depends on the distance to the wall ($y$), making it difficult to implement for complex geometries. Furthermore, this damping leads to an eddy-viscosity scaling of $O(y^2)$, which is inconsistent with the correct theoretical behavior of $O(y^3)$ \cite{wale}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{WALE model}
\label{sec:WALE}

The WALE model addresses the limitations of Smagorinsky one, while maintaining its advantageous properties of independence of coordinate transformations and grid topology choice.

Considering the traceless symmetric part of the square of the velocity gradient tensor:
\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{S}_{ij} = \frac{1}{2} (\,\widetilde{g}_{ij}^{\,2} + \widetilde{g}_{ji}^{\,2}\,) - \frac{1}{3} \, \delta_{ij} \, \widetilde{g}_{kk}^{\,2}
	\label{eq:wale-s-tensor}
\end{equation}
where the terms:
\begin{equation}
	\widetilde{g}_{ij} = \frac{\partial \, \widetilde{u}_i}{\partial \, x_j} = \widetilde{e}_{ij} + \widetilde{\omega}_{ij}
	\qquad \quad
	\widetilde{g}_{ij}^{\,2} = \frac{\partial \, \widetilde{u}_i}{\partial \, x_k} \, \frac{\partial \, \widetilde{u}_k}{\partial \, x_j} = (\widetilde{e}_{ik} + \widetilde{\omega}_{ik})(\widetilde{e}_{kj} + \widetilde{\omega}_{kj})
\end{equation}
Developing the square of the tensor defined in equation \eqref{eq:wale-s-tensor}, the scalar $\mathcal{S}_{ij} \, \mathcal{S}_{ij}$ is obtained:
\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{S}_{ij} \, \mathcal{S}_{ij} = \frac{1}{6} \, (\widetilde{e}^{\,2} \, \widetilde{e}^{\,2} + \widetilde{\omega}^{\,2} \, \widetilde{\omega}^{\,2}) + \frac{2}{3} \, \widetilde{e}^{\,2} \, \widetilde{\omega}^{\,2} + 2 \, IV_{e\omega}
	\label{eq:wale-invariant}
\end{equation}
In which the participating components are:
\begin{itemize}
	\item The squared magnitude of the resolved strain rate: $\widetilde{e}^{\,2} = \widetilde{e}_{ij} \, \widetilde{e}_{ij}$
	\item The squared magnitude of the resolved rotation rate: $\widetilde{\omega}^{\,2} = \widetilde{\omega}_{ij} \, \widetilde{\omega}_{ij}$
	\item A strain-rotation interaction term: $IV_{e\omega} = \widetilde{e}_{ik} \, \widetilde{e}_{kj} \, \widetilde{\omega}_{jl} \,\widetilde{\omega}_{li}$ .
\end{itemize}
The formulated invariant \eqref{eq:wale-invariant}, is capable of detecting turbulent structures dominated by either strain, rotation, or both. Moreover, in the case of pure shear (e.g., laminar Poiseuille flow), the strain and rotation magnitudes balance each other ($\widetilde{e}^{\,2} \approx \widetilde{\omega}^{\,2}$), causing the invariant to vanish. This property ensures that the sub-grid dissipation is naturally zero in laminar wall-bounded regions, allowing the development of instabilities and thus enabling the correct prediction of the laminar-to-turbulent transition.

At this point some corrections are implemented to ensure the correct  $O(y^3)$ near-wall scaling, dimensional coherency and to avoid numerical instabilities related to the cancellation of the denominator. Finally, the expression for the WALE model is the following:
\begin{equation}
	\nu_R = (C_W \Delta_F)^2 \, \frac{(\mathcal{S}_{ij} \, \mathcal{S}_{ij})^{\,3/2}}{(\widetilde{e}_{ij} \, \widetilde{e}_{ij})^{\, 5/2} + (\mathcal{S}_{ij} \, \mathcal{S}_{ij})^{\,5/4}}
	\label{eq:wale_viscosity}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Wall modeling}
While Sub-Grid Scale models provide the necessary closure for the unresolved turbulent structures within the bulk of the fluid domain, as seen in Section \ref{sec:wall-bounded-flows}, the treatment of solid boundaries in Large Eddy Simulations introduces further mathematical constraints. As the Reynolds number increases, the characteristic length scales of the near-wall turbulent structures (the viscous sub-layer and the buffer layer) become progressively smaller. Resolving these scales explicitly would require a grid resolution computationally prohibitive, effectively nullifying the advantages of the LES approach over DNS. 

When the grid is too coarse to capture the intense velocity gradients at the wall, in order to mathematically close the governing equations at the domain boundaries, a Wall Model (WM) is introduced. The wall model acts as a bridging function, connecting the resolved fluid flow variables sampled at a certain distance from the wall (in the outer region) to the local wall shear stress, bypassing the need to numerically resolve the inner layer.

\subsubsection{Reichardt equilibrium wall model}

Among the various algebraic formulations available to model the near-wall velocity profile, the extended law of the wall proposed by Reichardt \cite{reichardt} was selected for this study. Unlike classical wall laws, introduced in Section \ref{sec:equilibrium-momentum-balance}, that are valid only in well-defined sub-layers of the boundary layer, the considered wall model is based on empirical data and attempts to cover the entire flow, from the viscous sub-layer to the bulk/wake region. However, it is important to remark that, despite its extended spatial validity, Reichardt's formulation remains fundamentally an equilibrium wall model, relying on the assumption of a fully developed and attached turbulent boundary layer.

The continuous velocity profile is mathematically expressed by the following relationship:
\begin{equation}
    U^+ = \frac{1}{\kappa} \ln(1 + \kappa y^+) + C \left[ 1 - e^{-\frac{y^+}{B_1}} - \frac{y^+}{B_1} e^{-\frac{y^+}{B_2}} \right]
    \label{eq:reichardt}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa$ represents the Von Kármán constant, the terms $C$, $B_1$, and $B_2$ are empirical calibration parameters that govern the shape of the velocity profile in the buffer layer and ensure the smooth transition toward the logarithmic region.

% Add if NEQBL ODE model have to be introduced.
% While providing a robust analytical framework, the application of this algebraic model in WM-LES introduces specific numerical challenges \cite{reichardt-limitations}: The accuracy of the predicted mean wall shear stress is highly sensitive to the wall-normal distance of the velocity sampling point. Furthermore, the optimal values of the model parameters ($C, \kappa, B_1, B_2$) required to minimize the prediction error are strictly dependent on the chosen sampling height. Even when these parameters are carefully calibrated to accurately predict the mean stress, the static nature of the coefficients inherently limits the model's ability to properly capture the turbulent fluctuations of the wall friction velocity.

\chapter{Numerical framework}
\label{sec:numerical-framework}
\section{CaNS solver}
The numerical framework used is based on \textit{CaNS (Canonical Navier-Stokes)}, an open-source solver originally developed by Costa \cite{cans-costa} for massively parallel Direct Numerical Simulations (DNS) of canonical flows. The baseline code provides a highly efficient architecture for solving the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations on structured Cartesian grids. However, to address the high Reynolds number flows and complex geometries characterizing the present research, the original solver required specific adaptations. Consequently, the code was modified to operate as a Wall-Modeled Large Eddy Simulation (WM-LES) solver, utilizing implicit filtering and the WALE sub-grid scale model (introduced respectively in Sections \ref{sec:filtering} and \ref{sec:WALE}), coupled with an Immersed Boundary Method (IBM).

The algorithm solves the governing filtered Navier-Stokes equations presented in equation \eqref{eq:les-mom-equation} with unit density. To describe the time-integration scheme, it is convenient to recast the momentum equation by grouping the convective (or advective) and diffusive terms into a single operator, denoted here as $\mathcal{AD}(\bm{\widetilde{u}})$:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:AD-def}
    \mathcal{AD}(\bm{\widetilde{u}}) = - \nabla \left( \bm{\widetilde{u}} \, \bm{\widetilde{u}} \right)+ \nabla \cdot \left[ (\nu + \nu_{R}) \, \left(\nabla \bm{\widetilde{u}} + \left( \nabla \bm{\widetilde{u}} \, \right)^T \right) \right]
\end{equation}
Using this notation, the system of equations solved by the algorithm simplifies to:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:LES-AD}
\begin{cases}
    \nabla \cdot \bm{\widetilde{u}} = 0 \\[1.5ex]
    \dfrac{\partial \, \bm{\widetilde{u}}}{\partial t} = \mathcal{AD}(\bm{\widetilde{u}}) - \nabla \widetilde{p} + \bm{f}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
\textit{Note that from this point onward, since the (later described) Finite Volume Method deals by definition with volume-averaged values, the tilde notation for filtered variables is suppressed to simplify the discrete formulation.}

The core of the solver workflow is a three-stage Runge-Kutta (RK3) scheme for time integration, with a finite-volume pressure-correction method for the coupling of the mass and momentum Navier-Stokes equations for each sub-step.

\subsection{Time integration - RK3}
Runge–Kutta schemes perform time advancement by evaluating the solution at step $n+1$ via a sequence of intermediate stages. Considering a generic problem and a standard three-stage Runge-Kutta method:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:RK3-standard}
\begin{cases}
    \bm{u}_1 = \bm{u}_n \\[1.5ex]
    
    \bm{u}_2 = \bm{u}_n + \Delta t \, a_{21} \, \left( RHS_1 \right) \\[1.5ex]
    
    \bm{u}_3 = \bm{u}_n + \Delta t \, \{ a_{31} \, \left( RHS_1 \right) + a_{32} \, \left( RHS_2 \right) \} \\[1.5ex]
    
    \bm{u}_{n+1} = \bm{u}_n + \Delta t \, \sum_{k=1}^{3} b_k \, \left( RHS_k \right)
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
The last equation yields the $n+1$ field with a weighted average of the intermediate solutions found in each $k$-sub-step.

The use of multiple sub-steps allows for a significant increase in accuracy and stability, but at the same time the storage of the arrays for each stage causes an excessive memory consumption. For this reason, low-storage versions of the scheme are usually implemented. Unlike the standard formulation, the low-storage scheme updates the solution recursively "in-place":
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:RK3-low-storage}
    \bm{u}_{k+1} = \bm{u}_{k} + \Delta t \, \{ \alpha_k \, \left( RHS_{k} \right) + \beta_k \, \left( RHS_{k-1} \right) \}
\end{equation}
it is worth noting that for the first sub-step ($k=1$), the coefficient $\beta_1$ is zero, reducing the dependence solely to the initial state. This recursive structure allows the solver to operate using a minimal number of memory registers, by overwriting the solution array at each stage.

Using the time integration scheme as is, to solve the \eqref{eq:LES-AD} momentum equation alone is not possible, since both the mass and momentum conservation have to be imposed. In order to couple the four equations, and ensure a divergence-free velocity, the solver employs a pressure-correction algorithm, nested in each sub-step of the RK3 cycle.

\subsection{Pressure-correction}
The algorithm can be summarized as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Prediction:} The procedure begins with the computation of a predicted velocity field, denoted as $\bm{u}^*$, which is not necessarily divergence-free. This intermediate field is obtained by advancing the momentum equation using the convective and diffusive terms $\mathcal{AD}$ evaluated at current and previous stages, along with the pressure gradient and body forces term. Consistent with the low-storage RK3 scheme described in equation \eqref{eq:RK3-low-storage}, the prediction step is formulated as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:PC-predictor}
\bm{u}^* = \bm{u}_k + \Delta t \left( \alpha_k \, \mathcal{AD}_k + \beta_k \,\mathcal{AD}_{k-1} + \gamma_k \left( -\nabla p_{k-1/2} + \bm{f} \right) \right)
\end{equation}
% RICONTROLLARE
It is crucial to observe the time-centering of the pressure term. In this formulation, the pressure is staggered in time with respect to the velocity field. The term $\nabla p_{k-1/2}$ represents the pressure gradient evaluated at the mid-point of the previous interval.

The discrete form of Equation \eqref{eq:PC-predictor} is implemented as follows:
\begin{codebox}{src/rk.f90}
\begin{lstlisting}[style=mdfortran]
factor1 = rkpar(1) * dt
factor2 = rkpar(2) * dt
factor12 = factor1 + factor2
...
u(i,j,k) = u(i,j,k) + factor1 * dudtrk(i,j,k) 
	+ factor2 * dudtrko(i,j,k) 
	+ factor12 * (bforce(1) - dli(1) * (p(i+1,j,k) - p(i,j,k)))
v(i,j,k) = v(i,j,k) + factor1 * dvdtrk(i,j,k) 
	+ factor2 * dvdtrko(i,j,k) 
	+ factor12 * (bforce(2) - dli(2) * (p(i,j+1,k) - p(i,j,k)))
w(i,j,k) = w(i,j,k) + factor1 * dwdtrk(i,j,k) 
	+ factor2 * dwdtrko(i,j,k) 
	+ factor12 * (bforce(3) - dzci(k) * (p(i,j,k+1) - p(i,j,k)))
\end{lstlisting}
\end{codebox}
where, for instance, \texttt{dudtrk} and \texttt{dudtrko} are the the $x$-direction convective/diffusive terms at the current and previous (older) sub-steps.

\item \textbf{Poisson equation resolution:} Since, as anticipated, the evaluated $\bm{u}^*$ does not satisfy the continuity equation, it requires a correction. Such correction is enforced with a proper pressure correction field $p^* = p_{k+1/2} - p_{k-1/2}$, to obtain the final divergence free velocity of the current sub-step $\bm{u}_{k+1}$:
\begin{equation}
	\bm{u}_{k+1} - \bm{u}^* = - \Delta t \, \gamma_k \, \nabla p^*
	\label{eq:PC-v-correction-1}
\end{equation}
Applying the divergence to both sides:
\begin{equation}
	0 - \nabla \cdot \bm{u}^* = - \Delta t \, \gamma_k \, \nabla^2 p^* 
	\qquad \implies \qquad
	\nabla^2 p^* = \dfrac{\nabla \cdot \bm{u}^*}{\gamma_k \, \Delta t}
\end{equation}
The solution of the Poisson equation is usually the most computationally intensive step of a solver. In \textit{CaNS}, a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) based method is employed, applied along the $x$ and $y$ domain dimensions \cite{cans-costa}.

\item \textbf{Velocity correction:} The divergence-free velocity at the subsequent step $\bm{u}_{k+1}$ can be retrieved by reconsidering equation \eqref{eq:PC-v-correction-1}, that becomes:
\begin{equation}
	\bm{u}_{k+1} = \bm{u}^* - \Delta t \, \gamma_k \, \nabla p^*
\end{equation}

\item \textbf{Pressure update:} The pressure field at step $k+1/2$ is obtained by summation:
\begin{equation}
	p_{k+1/2} = p_{k-1/2} + p^*
\end{equation}

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Finite Volume Method}
\label{sec:FVM}
The previous sections described the time integration scheme and the pressure correction algorithm. To complete the description, it is necessary to define how the spatial terms of equation \eqref{eq:LES-AD} are discretized.

The solver uses a standard second-order Finite Volume Method (FVM) on a structured Cartesian grid. This approach is based on the integral form of the conservation laws applied to elementary control volumes. The main advantage of this method is that it naturally ensures the conservation of mass and momentum fluxes across the control volume faces \cite{cfd-anderson}. For this reason the method is often implemented for its ability to compute flow fields which present discontinuities (for instance shocks, slip lines), namely weak solutions \cite{cfd-blazek}.

To obtain the integral form of the governing equations, the Gauss's Divergence Theorem is applied. This theorem allows to transform the volume integral into a surface integral, which can be subsequently approximated by a summation over the faces of the discrete control volume. Considering a generic field, for instance $\bm{u}$, the continuous and discrete forms of the theorem are recalled below:
\begin{equation}
    \int_{\Omega} \left( \nabla \cdot \bm{\widetilde{u}} \right) \, d\Omega 
    = 
    \int_{S} \bm{\widetilde{u}} \cdot \, d \bm{S}
    \quad \implies \quad
    \sum_{f} \bm{u_f} \cdot \bm{S}_{f}
    = 
    \sum_{f} \bm{F}_f^{\, vel} \cdot \bm{S}_{f}
\end{equation}
where, in this example, $\bm{S}$ is the surface vector, $\left( \bm{u_f} \cdot \bm{S}_{f} \right)$ is the flux contribution across the face $f$, and $\bm{F}_f^{\, vel} \equiv \bm{u_f}$ is the (velocity) flux density again across the face $f$. Note that the pedix $f$ act as the position of evaluation of fluid quantities.

Integrating \eqref{eq:LES-AD} in a cell control volume $\Omega$ and applying the divergence theorem:
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		\int_{\Omega} \dfrac{\partial \, \bm{\widetilde{u}}}{\partial t} \, d \Omega =
		&- \int_S \left( \bm{\widetilde{u}} \, \bm{\widetilde{u}} \right) \cdot \bm{n} \, dS
		+ \int_S (\nu + \nu_{R}) \, \left(\nabla \bm{\widetilde{u}} + \left( \nabla \bm{\widetilde{u}} \, \right)^T \right) \, dS \\
		&- \int_S \left( \widetilde{p} \, \bm{I} \right) \, dS
		+ \int_\Omega \bm{f} \, d \Omega
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
This equation can be rewritten in its semi-discretized form, considering $\Omega = \Delta x \, \Delta y \, \Delta z$, as:
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		\phantom{,}
		\left(\Delta x \, \Delta y \, \Delta z \right) \, \dfrac{\partial \, \bm{u}}{\partial t} =
		&- \sum_f \left( \bm{u} \, \bm{u} \right)_f \cdot \bm{S}_f \\
		&+ \sum_f \left[ (\nu + \nu_{R}) \, \left(\nabla \bm{u} + \left( \nabla \bm{u} \, \right)^T \right) \right]_f \cdot \bm{S}_f \\
		&- \sum_f \left( p \, \bm{I} \right) \cdot \bm{S}_f \\
		&+ \left(\Delta x \, \Delta y \, \Delta z \right) \, \bm{f}
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
That is, in indexed notation:
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:discretized-mom-indexed}
\begin{split}
    (\Delta x \, \Delta y \, \Delta z) \, \dfrac{\partial \, u_i}{\partial t} =
    &- \sum_f (u_i \, u_j)_f \, S_{f,j} \\
    & + \sum_f \left[ (\nu + \nu_{R}) \, \left( \nabla_j \, u_i + \nabla_i \, u_j \right) \right]_f \, S_{f,j} \\
    & - \sum_f p_f \, S_{f,i} \\
    & + (\Delta x \, \Delta y \, \Delta z) \, f_i
\end{split}
\end{equation}
The flux density terms appearing can be identified. Specifically, the convective and viscous flux density tensors are defined as:
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
    &F_{i, j, f}^{\, conv} = (u_i \, u_j)_f \\
    &F_{i, j, f}^{\, visc} = \left[ (\nu + \nu_{R}) \, \left( \nabla_j u_i + \nabla_i u_j \right) \right]_f
\end{split}
\label{eq:conv-visc-fluxes}
\end{equation}
where the index $i$ express the direction in which the momentum conservation equation is considered, $j$ indicates the flux vector component direction and $f$ the position in which the tensor is evaluated. Since the considered grid is Cartesian, the considered face $f$ already determines the considered flux direction, so that the $j$ index is redundant and can be suppressed.

Finally, to bridge the theoretical formulation with the numerical implementation, the specific notation adopted in the code is introduced. A distinction is made between the generic tensor indices and the specific spatial directions: while the lowercase subscripts ($i, j, k$) act as placeholders, the uppercase letters $I, J, K$ represent the considered direction corresponding to the $x, y$, and $z$ axes, respectively. Furthermore, the control volume faces are identified by their orientation relative to the coordinate system, using $P$ (Plus) for the positive direction and $M$ (Minus) for the negative direction.

For instance, considering the momentum balance \eqref{eq:discretized-mom-indexed} in the $x \equiv I$ direction:
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		(\Delta x \, \Delta y \, \Delta z) \, \dfrac{\partial \, u}{\partial t} =
		&- (F_{I, IP}^{\, conv} - F_{I, IM}^{\, conv})(\Delta y \, \Delta z)
			-  (F_{I, JP}^{\, conv} - F_{I, JM}^{\, conv})(\Delta x \, \Delta z)\\
			&-  (F_{I, KP}^{\, conv} - F_{I, KM}^{\, conv})(\Delta x \, \Delta y)\\
		&+ (F_{I, IP}^{\, visc} - F_{I, IM}^{\, visc})(\Delta y \, \Delta z)
			+  (F_{I, JP}^{\, visc} - F_{I, JM}^{\, visc})(\Delta x \, \Delta z)\\
			&+  (F_{I, KP}^{\, visc} - F_{I, KM}^{\, visc})(\Delta x \, \Delta y)\\
		&- (p_{JP} - p_{JM})(\Delta x \, \Delta z) + (\Delta x \, \Delta y \, \Delta z) \, f_J 
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
And simplifying the volume:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
		\phantom{(\Delta x \, \Delta y \, ,)}\,
        \dfrac{\partial \, u}{\partial t} =
        &- \dfrac{F_{I, IP}^{\, conv} - F_{I, IM}^{\, conv}}{\Delta x}
         - \dfrac{F_{I, JP}^{\, conv} - F_{I, JM}^{\, conv}}{\Delta y}
         - \dfrac{F_{I, KP}^{\, conv} - F_{I, KM}^{\, conv}}{\Delta z} \\[1em]
        &+ \dfrac{F_{I, IP}^{\, visc} - F_{I, IM}^{\, visc}}{\Delta x}
         + \dfrac{F_{I, JP}^{\, visc} - F_{I, JM}^{\, visc}}{\Delta y}
         + \dfrac{F_{I, KP}^{\, visc} - F_{I, KM}^{\, visc}}{\Delta z} \\[1em]
        &- \dfrac{p_{JP} - p_{JM}}{\Delta y} + f_J 
    \end{split}
	\label{eq:discretized-mom-faces}
\end{equation}
To visualize the components acting in the momentum conservation equation, the convective and viscous flux density vector, along with the pressure term, are represented in Figure \ref{fig:fvm-flux-components}. As shown, they are consistently aligned with the positive coordinate axes on both faces, while pressure ($p$) acts compressively, directed inward.

    \label{fig:fvm-flux-components}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Flux computation on staggered grid}
The evaluation of the flux density terms defined in the previous section requires the knowledge of fluxes and pressures at specific locations on the grid, specifically at the centers of the control volume faces. However, the solver employs a staggered grid arrangement, to prevent instabilities and spurious oscillations.

In this configuration the grid flow variables (from which the convective and diffusive fluxes are derived), are stored at different nodal locations. Scalar quantities (pressure $p$, viscosity $\nu$, ...) are defined at the grid cell centers, while velocity components are stored at the midpoints of the cell faces corresponding to their direction. Consequently, the control volumes for the momentum equations are shifted relative to the grid cells, as illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:fvm-stencils}.

Since the variable values are not directly available at all the required face locations for the flux computation, some basic interpolation schemes (arithmetic averaging) are implemented.
The evaluation of first and second derivatives instead, is performed through a Centered First Derivative (CFD/CS2) stencil, accurate to the second order. Applying such interpolation or discretization to the fluxes terms \eqref{eq:conv-visc-fluxes} that appears in equation \eqref{eq:discretized-mom-faces} yields, for example:
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		&F_{I, IP}^{\, conv} = \left( \dfrac{u_{pcc} + u_{ccc}}{2} \right)^2 \\[0.5em]
		&F_{I, JP}^{\, visc} = \left( \nu + \dfrac{s_{ccc}+s_{pcc}+s_{cpc}+s_{ppc}}{4} \right) \left( \dfrac{u_{cpc}-u_{ccc}}{\Delta x} + \dfrac{v_{pcc}-v_{ccc}}{\Delta x} \right)
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
And the corresponding code implementation for the $\mathcal{AD}$ contribution is the following:

\section{Immersed Boundary Method}
The adoption of the Immersed Boundary Method (IBM) is primarily driven by the necessity to overcome the limitations and computational costs associated with traditional body-fitted grids. 

Specifically, the conventional approach employs structured or unstructured grids that conform to the geometry. The mesh generation requires defining a curvilinear coordinate system for the governing equations, aligned with the grid or to incorporate the information regarding the grid in the discretization \cite{ibm-mittal-iaccarino}. Such operations require complex and computationally demanding algorithms that can result in poor quality discretizations (skewed elements, for instance), potentially compromising numerical stability. The variability of the considered geometry and the resulting mesh, makes the problem of storing and accessing the data related to each element non-optimal in terms of performance and non-straightforward in its implementation. Furthermore, in the presence of moving boundaries, the solver requires a continuous re-meshing.

The most significant advantage of the IBM lies in the simplification of the grid generation phase. The IBM employs structured Cartesian grids, facilitating the implementation of efficient iterative solvers and simplifying parallelization. Such grids are defined independently from the geometry and fixed in time, meaning that the body can move or deform without requiring computationally expensive re-meshing steps.

    \label{fig:ibm-demo}

\subsection{Geometry representation}
In order to use the IBM there is the need to numerically describe the immersed geometry. The STereoLithography (STL) file format is an industry standard for the representation of 3D objects. It describes an unstructured triangulated surface by the unit normal and vertices. However, since the solver operates on a structured Cartesian grid, a mapping strategy is required to transfer the geometrical information from the imported surface to the grid. The standard approach to perform such transfer is through the generation of a Signed Distance Field (SDF).

The SDF is a scalar field, defined for every grid node, representing the shortest distance to the immersed boundary. Furthermore, a sign is attributed to distinguish between the fluid (positive SDF) and the solid (negative SDF) domains. This approach is particularly efficient for several reasons: it directly provides the wall distance information at each node, which is necessary for the wall modeling; being a continuous field, it allows for a sub-grid resolution of the geometry and an accurate determination of the interface position between nodes; it also simplifies the calculation of the local wall-normal direction, from the SDF gradient.

Despite these advantages, the computation of the SDF is a non-trivial operation. The evaluation of the exact distance between the grid nodes and the unstructured surface mesh can be computationally expensive and time-consuming, especially when dealing with complex geometries and high-resolution grids. The initial phase of the research relied on a parallelized code based on a direct search algorithm. However, to overcome the performance limitations of this approach, a new dedicated pre-processing software has been developed in the context of this work. This tool features a Graphical User Interface (GUI) to assist the user in the setup phase and implements optimized algorithms to efficiently generate the distance field. The standard generation approach and the proposed optimized process will be discussed in detail in the following sections.

\subsubsection{Direct search algorithm}
The first strategy adopted for the SDF evaluation was based on a Fortran code to directly evaluate the geometric distance and sign for each point of the grid. Its workflow can be briefly summarized as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Domain decomposition:} The computational domain is divided into slices, each one assigned to an MPI process.
	\item \textbf{Distance calculation:} The algorithm iterates over every point of the staggered grid. For each one, a loop is performed over all the triangles of the STL object to calculate the distance, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:direct-search}. The minimum value is stored.
	\item \textbf{Sign determination:} To determine if each point is inside or outside the body and the corresponding sign of the SDF, a ray is cast from the grid node towards an internal reference point (the centroid in this case). The sign is computed based on the even or odd number of intersections with the geometry surface.
\end{enumerate}

    \caption{Direct search approach schematization.}

While this approach is intuitive and straightforward, it suffers from scalability issues. The computational load of the algorithm in fact, is proportional to $O(N_{grid} \times N_{triangles})$. In order to implement a more efficient search algorithm, and introduce a visual interface for geometry positioning and scaling inside the computational domain, a dedicated pre-processing software was developed.

\subsubsection{Optimized pre-processing tool}
The pre-processor was developed in Python, implementing the Open3D library for high-performance geometric computations, PyQt5 for the Graphical User Interface and PyVista for the real-time 3D window rendering.

The operational workflow of the developed tool can be divided into four main stages:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Project import:} At first the interface, depicted in Figure \ref{fig:sdf-generator}, allows for the project folder selection, inside of which the 3D object STL file and the simulation and grid input parameters NML file are automatically identified and loaded. The domain bounding box and the 3D geometry are then visually rendered in the right window and they can be inspected using interactive 3D navigation.
	\item \textbf{Immersed geometry positioning:} A dedicated left panel was introduced to allow for the scaling, translation and rotation of the immersed geometry and to activate/deactivate the visualization of the staggered grid nodes on the 3D view.
	\item \textbf{Ray-casting:} Once the immersed geometry is positioned as desired, it is possible to proceed with the SDF computation. This operation is performed by the function \texttt{compute\_signed\_distance()} provided by the \texttt{RaycastingScene} class in Open3D. As detailed in the official documentation \cite{open3D-doc}, this module employs the Intel Embree library, which in turn relies on the Bounding Volume Hierarchy (BVH) approach \cite{embree-doc}.
	
	The BVH approach effectively optimizes the search pattern, organizing the geometry into a tree-based structure of nested bounding volumes. During the distance query (or ray-casting) the algorithm checks if the parent volumes (represented in green in Figure \ref{fig:ray-casting-bvh}) are intersected. If they are, the search is narrowed in the sub-tree volumes (represented in red), otherwise the entire related sub-tree is directly discarded, reducing the number of required measures to $O(N_{grid} \cdot \log N_{triangles})$, as schematized.

This log contains the SHA256 hashes of each input and output file, ensuring the reproducibility, traceability, and integrity of the pre-processing stage.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Immersed boundary forcing}
As introduced above, the IBM consists of the immersion of the generic geometry in a fixed Cartesian grid. The approach relies on the resolution of the entire domain, without the need of excluding the geometry from it. The immersed body presence, instead, has to be enforced on the flow field by introducing some sort of modification, or forcing, in the governing equations. This can be done in two different ways \cite{ibm-mittal-iaccarino}:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Continuous forcing:} The continuous forcing approach is based on the introduction of a forcing function in the continuous governing equations \eqref{eq:LES-AD} before their discretization. This is particularly useful for elastic boundary cases, with possible applications for example in biological and multiphase flows. On the other hand, the introduction of parameters for boundary description can have implications in terms of numerical stability and the smoothing of the forcing function results in a not sharp interface representation.
	\item \textbf{Discrete forcing:} With the discrete forcing approach, the equations \eqref{eq:LES-AD} are first discretized on the Cartesian grid, as already discussed in Section \ref{sec:FVM}, and the discretization itself is adjusted near the walls to take into account the body presence. This direct control over the discretized equations allows for a better control also of numerical stability and properties conservation of the solver. Moreover, a sharper representation of the interfaces can be obtained, and this is particularly relevant, especially at high Reynolds numbers.
\end{itemize}

Following the classification above, the present code adopts a discrete forcing approach. The interaction between the fluid and the solid body is handled through a two-step procedure involving the solid nodes and the fluid-solid interface:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Solid nodes:} The velocity field in the solid domain (SDF $\leq$ 0) is set to zero. This is done at each sub-step of the Runge-Kutta time integration, before the start of the pressure-correction update.
	\item \textbf{Interface flux correction:} Since the convective/viscous term ($\mathcal{AD}$) is computed in the entire domain without taking into account the effect of the wall presence, it is necessary to correct/update the solution in the fluid (SDF > 0) near-wall region. This requires, first, choosing a criterion to define whether each control volume belongs to such a region; then for each interface control volume the velocity field is sampled, at a specific distance and direction with respect to the wall; the wall model computes the local wall stress; and finally the viscous term calculated by the solver is substituted in the momentum balance by the updated viscous term, accounting for the local wall stress.
\end{enumerate}

While the first step is trivial in its implementation, for the interface flux correction multiple criteria and approaches can be used, with different levels of accuracy, complexity and performance. 

The method originally implemented in this framework, was based on a approximated face-based check for wall intersection and stress computation. In the current work, a more accurate approach was implemented and tested, based instead on the calculation of the exact intersection area between each control volume and the geometry (wetted area or cut-cell method).

\subsubsection{Face-based staircase forcing}
The first approach employed is represented in Figure \ref{fig:ibm-forcing-staircase} in 2D. The entire process is repeated for each of the staggered grids. At first a near-wall layer of control volumes is identified: this is done by defining, for each node, a distance $d$, consisting of the projection of the related cell along the local wall-normal direction. This allows to obtain a reduced subset of nodes (pruning) to be considered in the actual interface detection. For every node inside this layer, a related matching point is localized at a distance proportional to $d$, along the wall-normal direction, there the 3D external velocity field is sampled by interpolation, obtaining the $\bm{u}_{MP}$ vector. The wall-parallel component of the external velocity $\bm{u}_{MP \, \parallel}$ and the sampling distance are fed as inputs for the wall model, that computes and returns the scalar wall stress value $\tau_w$.

To construct the wall stress vector and tensor, for each node considered a local orthonormal and geometry-aligned reference system is defined. This frame consists of a wall-normal versor $\bm{\hat{n}}$, alongside with a tangent $\bm{\hat{t}}$ and a binormal $\bm{\hat{b}}$ versors lying on the wall-parallel plane. This allows the decomposition of the scalar wall stress along the $\bm{\hat{t}}$ and $\bm{\hat{b}}$ directions, yielding the components $\tau_{w,t}$ and $\tau_{w,b}$, so that the resulting stress vector is aligned to the sampled external velocity direction:
\begin{equation}
	\bm{\tau}_w = \tau_{w,t} \cdot \bm{\hat{t}} + \tau_{w,b} \cdot \bm{\hat{b}}
\end{equation}
In order to mathematically represent the three-dimensional stress state that the wall induces in the fluid, the wall stress tensor in the local frame of reference is constructed as:
\begin{equation}
	\bm{\tau}_{w, \, local} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & \tau_{w,t} \\ 0 & 0 & \tau_{w,b} \\ \tau_{w,t} & \tau_{w,b} & 0 \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
Subsequently, to be correctly applied as a forcing term, the local stress tensor is transformed back into the global Cartesian coordinate system, through a rotation matrix $\bm{R}$:
\begin{equation}
	\bm{\tau}_{w, \, global} = \mathbf{R} \, (\bm{\tau}_{w, \, local}) \, \mathbf{R}^T = \begin{bmatrix} t_x & b_x & n_x \\ t_y & b_y & n_y \\ t_z & b_z & n_z \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & \tau_{w,t} \\ 0 & 0 & \tau_{w,b} \\ \tau_{w,t} & \tau_{w,b} & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} t_x & t_y & t_z \\ b_x & b_y & b_z \\ n_x & n_y & n_z \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
The stress tensor in the global frame of reference $\bm{\tau}_{w, \, global}$ contains the components:
\begin{equation}
	\bm{\tau}_{w, \, global} = 
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\sigma_{xx} & \tau_{xy} & \tau_{xz} \\
		\tau_{xy} & \sigma_{yy} & \tau_{yz} \\
		\tau_{xz} & \tau_{yz} & \sigma_{zz}
	\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
where the subscripts follow the standard convention to identify the orientation of the surface on which the stress acts and the direction of the stress component. For example, $\sigma_{yy}$ is the normal stress acting on a surface whose normal is aligned with the $y$-axis, and directed along the $y$-axis; while $\tau_{yx}$ is the shear stress acting on a surface whose normal is aligned with the $y$-axis, but directed along the $x$-axis.
%[commented out]The Cauchy's tetrahedron argument explains how knowing the stress vectors acting on three mutually orthogonal faces of an (infinitesimal) element allow to determine the entire generic stress state \cite{lenci}. In this case the generic stress state is generated by the shear stress vector $\bm{\tau}_w$ in the generic $(\bm{\hat{t}},\, \bm{\hat{b}})$ plane. Moreover, the computed $\bm{\tau}_{w, \, global}$ contains the three stress vectors acting on the three (mutually orthogonal) Cartesian faces. Thus, according to the theorem, it is possible to represent the arbitrarily-oriented wall stress, with a system of normal and shear stresses defined at the Cartesian faces. [commented out]

At this point the stress tensor in the global frame of reference is known at each near-wall node and has to be enforced. The interface is detected by checking whether any of the adjacent cells sharing a face with these nodes lie within the solid domain, which is indicated by a change in the sign of the SDF. If this condition is met, the shared cell face is considered part of the approximated interface. As shown in Figure \ref{fig:ibm-forcing-staircase-B}, with this approach the approximated interface assumes a staircase shape. 

For each face of the staircase interface, the stress component that both acts on this face and is directed along the considered staggered grid direction, is multiplied by the corresponding Cartesian face area. The resulting contribution is then used to update the viscous term in the momentum balance of the corresponding fluid cell.

The staircase method provides an highly practical solution and a straightforward implementation for the forcing problem. Since the stepped interface averagely follows the real one, the area overestimation related to the staircase approximation is mitigated by the use of the stress tensor. Despite these beneficial aspects, it is necessary to acknowledge the method limitations, that are primarily related to the spatial distibution of the wall stress contributions.

First, the staircase approximation introduces some form of spatial inhomogeneity: as shown in Figure \ref{fig:ibm-forcing-staircase-C} for the $u$-velocity grid, the wall shear stress component $\tau_{yx}$, acting on the horizontal faces, is reasonably distributed. However, the wall-normal stress component $\sigma_{xx}$, which physically should be smoothly distributed along the entire inclined section, is mathematically concentrated entirely on the vertical faces of cell 1 and 5.

Another aspect is the tolopogical mismatch between the exact physical geometry and the numerical boundary where the forcing is actually imposed. This misalignment complicates the post-processing phases, expecially when an integration of flow variables like pressure or friction drag is required.

\subsubsection{Cut-cell forcing}
\label{sec:cut-cell-forcing}
To improve the interface discretization, and obtain higher accuracy during the application of the sampled wall stress, a cut-cell forcing approach was developed. The idea is to pre-compute, for every control volume of the domain, the scalar value of the interface area intersected with the cell. Although this operation might appear computationally expensive, its optimized implementation proved to be almost negligible in terms of computational time. This efficiency can be explained at least by two aspects: the algorithm performs an initial pruning of the domain, discarding all the control volumes located too far from the interface. The cells with a non-null wetted area, that can now be considered interface cells by definition, represent only a small percentage of the total domain. The second aspect is that the intersected area is computed only once at the beginning of the simulation and then stored. Since the considered case studies involve static geometries, there is no need to recompute the wetted area at each time step. Nevertheless, the code is already structured to handle geometry updates through a simple call to the \texttt{IBM\_UpdateGeometry} subroutine, thus allowing straightforward extension to moving or deforming boundaries.

The algorithm for the wetted area computation locally represents the intersected wall as a plane, with a known normal direction and distance from the cell center (obtained from the SDF). Using an analytical approach, the exact positions of each intersection point along the cell edges are determined (they can range from 3 to 6). This procedure allows computing the area of the resulting polygon, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:ibm-forcing-wet-area}. The improved accuracy of the interface representation compared to the previous method (Figure \ref{fig:ibm-forcing-staircase-B}) can be observed in Figure \ref{fig:ibm-forcing-cut-cell-B}, where the exact and the approximated boundaries are almost indistinguishable due to their near-perfect overlap.

According to this new definition, the interface cells are no longer limited to the fluid domain, but they can be either solid or fluid, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:ibm-forcing-cut-cell}. The sampling procedure along the local normal direction remains unchanged, but it is now performed for both solid and fluid control volumes, resulting in the wall-stress tensor in global coordinates. Unlike the previous approach, it is now used the exact wetted area to evaluate the interface forces. Specifically, the friction force applied to the fluid is computed by a matrix multiplication between the wall-stress tensor in global coordinates, and the local normal vector. The resulting shear stress vector is then scaled by the exact intersected area evaluated for the specific cell.

As previously explained, also solid interface cells are now included in the friction force computation. However, since these control volumes lie within the solid body, the computed forcing terms cannot be applied directly to them. To overcome this issue, a flux redistribution strategy towards the surrounding fluid nodes is introduced. Specifically, the adjacent fluid cells (i.e., those sharing a face with the considered solid node) are selected as target control volumes for the redistribution, as illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:ibm-forcing-cut-cell-B}. To ensure that this procedure reflects the local flow physics as accurately as possible, a weighted approach was designed. The weighting factors are assigned according to two main criteria:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Distance from the wall}: higher priority is given to the fluid nodes located closer to the actual solid boundary. This requirement is necessary to maintain the physical proximity of the wall effect, preventing the boundary condition from being unphysically applied further away into the bulk of the fluid flow.
\item \textbf{Directional alignment}: this is evaluated as the angle between the local surface normal (originating from the solid node) and the position vector connecting the solid node center to the target fluid node center. By assigning higher weights to smaller angles, this criterion prevents an artificial spatial diffusion (or "smearing") of the local wall-stress contribution along the tangential direction of the interface.
\end{itemize}

At this point, the near-interface fluid control volumes collect the overall wall-stress effects. Specifically, cells in this region accumulates both their own direct boundary contribution (evaluated using their local wetted area, if intersected) and the redistributed forcing terms originating from the adjacent solid cells. This weighted aggregation is designed to be strictly conservative, ensuring that the total friction force exerted by the solid boundary on the fluid domain is rigorously preserved during the transfer process.

\section{Periodic channel}
The first test simulation performed is the canonical turbulent channel flow. The main purpose of this test case is to assess the correct implementation of the implemented cut-cell forcing and the proper functioning of the numerical framework in general. Despite its simple geometric configuration, the turbulent channel flow represents a fundamental benchmark to verify that the near-wall physics and the mean flow behavior are correctly captured by the numerical solver.

\subsection{Simulation setup}
\label{sec:periodic-channel-setup}
The geometry for the turbulent channel flow is defined with a streamwise lenght $L_x = 5 \text{ m}$, a channel height $L_y = 1 \text{ m}$ and a spanwise width $L_z = 2 \text{ m}$. The computational domain dimensions coincide with those of the geometry, except for the $y$-direction that is exteneded to $1.1 \text{ m}$ to include a portion of the walls and allow their detection by the IBM solver.

In the streamwise and spanwise directions, periodic boundary conditions are applied, with a syntethic noise generator to anticipate the laminar-to-turbulent transition at the beginning of the simulation. Since periodic conditions are imposed in the streamwise direction, the flow would naturally dissipate its kinetic energy. To counteract this momentum decay and maintain a constant mass-flow rate, the bulk forcing function is enabled with a bulk velocity target of $U_b = 1 \text{ m/s}$. The fluid viscosity is set in order to result in a bulk Reynolds number of $Re = 80000$.

A grid with resolution of $256 \times 96 \times 96$ is considered. The choice of the mesh must ensure that the simulation strictly operates within a Wall-Modeled Large Eddy Simulation (WM-LES) approach, avoiding the direct resolution of the boundary layer characteristic of a Wall-Resolved LES (WR-LES). To verify that the adopted spatial discretizations deliberately avoid this condition, it is necessary to compute an a priori evaluation of the wall units. Considering the Dean correlation \cite{dean-correlation}, based on empirical experimental data, to estimate the channel friction coefficient $C_f$:
\begin{equation}
	C_f = 0.073 \cdot Re^{-0.25} = 4.341 \cdot 10^{-3}
	\label{eq:dean-correlation}
\end{equation}
The wall stress can be calculated as:
\begin{equation}
	\tau_w = \dfrac{1}{2} \, \rho \, U_b^2 \, C_f =  2.170 \cdot 10^{-3} \text{ Pa}
\end{equation}
Consequently, the friction velocity result in:
\begin{equation}
	u_\tau = \sqrt{\dfrac{\tau_w}{\rho}} = U_b \, \sqrt{\dfrac{C_f}{2}} = 0.04659 \text{ m/s}
\end{equation}
Based on the wall laws introduced in Section \ref{sec:wall-laws}, the first cell position can be expressed in wall units as:
\begin{equation}
	\Delta y^+ = \dfrac{\Delta y \, u_\tau}{\nu} = \dfrac{((L_y + 0.1)/N_y) \, u_\tau}{\nu} = 42.70
\end{equation}
To obtain a first estimation of the sampling position for the wall model, considering it is sampled at a distance of 3 cells (projected in the wall-normal direction) from the interface:
\begin{equation}
	y_{MP}^+ = \dfrac{y_{MP} \, u_\tau}{\nu} = \dfrac{(3 \cdot \Delta y) \, u_\tau}{\nu} = 128.1
\end{equation}
This shows that the first cell position, and the sampling location, fall well above the viscous sub-layer.

The numerical simulation was carried out for a total duration of $350 \text{ s}$. To ensure that the considered data is completely independent of the initial conditions, an initial startup interval was selected and discarted. The selection was done by monitoring the evolution of the mean velocity profile. At approximately $t = 80 \text{ s}$ a statistically steady behavior was observed. However, to introduce a conservative safety margin, the collection of flow statistics was initiated at $t = 150 \text{ s}$. The remaining $200 \text{ s}$ of simulation provided a sufficiently wide temporal window to obtain properly converged mean profiles.

\subsection{Simulation setup}
The simulation setup consists of a channel with periodic constrictions at the bottom wall and a planar top wall. The precise shape of the lower boundary is analytically described by a piecewise-defined polynomial function, with a unit hill height $h_H=1 \text{ m}$. The physical domain dimensions were chosen according to the most common setups found in the literature, which assign a streamwise length $L_x = 9 \, h_H$, a channel height $L_y = 3.035 \, h_H$, and a spanwise width $L_z = 4.5 \, h_H$. To properly include the top and bottom walls allowing their detection by the Immersed Boundary Method, the computational domain is slightly extended in the $y$-direction, resulting in a height of $3.135 \, h_H$. However this doesn't affect the channel geometry, which maintain its height of $3.035 \, h_H$.

In terms of boundary conditions, periodicity is applied in both the streamwise and spanwise directions. The periodicity allows the flow to naturally develop and recycle turbulent structures within the computational domain. A velocity target of $U_b = 1 \text{ m/s}$ is set for the bulk velocity function. The simulation is performed at a bulk Reynolds number of $37000$, which is defined as:
\begin{equation}
	Re = \dfrac{U_b \, h_H}{\nu}
\end{equation}
In order to evaluate the mesh independence and the solver sensitivity to spatial resolution, two grids were selected for the discretization, with different cell counts ($N_x \times N_y \times N_z$) and aspect ratios, labeled as follows:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \texttt{PH-R1:} A coarser grid consisting of $128 \times 80 \times 80$ cells.
	\item \texttt{PH-R2:} A refined grid consisting of $256 \times 96 \times 128$ cells.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Bulk forcing}
\label{sec:bulk-forcing}
Since the periodic streamwise boundary conditions effectively simulate an infinite channel, there is the need to maintain the fluid flow in motion. The solver include a proper bulk forcing function, based on the average velocity of the entire fluid domain. When the computed average velocity diverges from the target value, a proportional contribution vector is directly added to the solution $\bm{u}^*$ at the prediction step of the pressure-correction scheme.

However, the standard periodic hills benchmark requires a different constraint: a constant mass-flow rate measured specifically at the cross-section located on top of the hill crest. In order to enforce and maintain the required flow rate, a dedicated bulk forcing approach was implemented, based on a Proportional-Integral (PI) control law.

At each time step $n$, the instantaneous spatially-averaged streamwise velocity $\langle u \rangle_{\text{inlet}}$ is evaluated at the inlet section through a surface integral. The discrepancy between the target bulk velocity $U_b$ and the measured value defines the instantaneous error: $\varepsilon_n \equiv U_b - \langle u_{\text{inlet}} \rangle_n$. To eliminate both the steady-state offset and transient oscillations, the streamwise volumetric forcing term $f_{f,x}$ is computed using a proportional gain $K_p$ and an integral gain $K_i$. The discrete time-integral of the error is updated at each time step as $\epsilon_n = \epsilon_{n-1} + \varepsilon_n \, \Delta t$, yielding the final driving force:
$f_{f,x} = K_p \, \varepsilon_n + K_i \, \epsilon_n$.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\centering
	\vspace{0.3cm}
	\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{img/PH-bulk-forcing.jpg} 
	\caption{Mean inflow mass-flow rate and forcing term applied in simulation \texttt{PH-R2}}
	\label{fig:PH-bulk-forcing}
	\vspace{0.15cm}
\end{figure}

This dynamically adjusted forcing term is then added to the RHS of the discretized momentum equation. The PI controller ensures that the prescribed mass-flow rate at the crest is smoothly maintained throughout the simulation.

As the time progresses to $t = 9 \cdot t_{FT}$, the macroscopic topology of the flow is already visible. However, the upper region of the channel presents large undulations. At $t = 23 \cdot t_{FT}$ a fully chaotic turbulent state is reached, where fine turbulent structures populate the entire fluid domain.

\subsection{Average velocity profiles}
The streamwise velocity profiles for simulations \texttt{PH-R1}, \texttt{PH-R2} along with the results available from other LES and experimental studies \cite{cinnella} \cite{manhart} \cite{manhart-profiles-LES}, extracted at various locations along the channel, are presented in Figure \ref{fig:PH-velocity-profiles}.

At the first position downstream the hill crest $x/h = 0.5$, the three profiles depict the start of the flow separation, with a region of negative streamwise velocity near the bottom wall. The backflow is poorly represented in the \texttt{PH-R1} case, due to its coarse discretization, conversely in \texttt{PH-R2}, the separation is even more marked than in the reference results. From that point, a region of negative streamwise velocity is visible near the bottom wall, defining the extent of the recirculation bubble. Moving further downstream, the near-wall velocity gradually recovers as the flow approaches the reattachment point. In the reference data, the flow reattaches at $x/h = 4.0$ and then the velocity near the lower boundary transitions back to positive values. Both the numerical simulations performed exhibit a longer recirculation region, and a reattachment point shifted downstream, at $x/h = 4.87$ for \texttt{PH-R1} and at $x/h = 5.26$ for \texttt{PH-R2}. Past the reattachment point, the flow undergoes a recovery phase over the remaining portion of domain.

\subsection{Results analysis}
The qualitative behavior of the flow, and in particular the presence of a reattachment region, represents an important result for the solver. In the complex configuration of the periodic hills, it was demonstrated that the capability of capturing the reattachment phenomenon is strongly influenced by the wall modeling, especially on coarse grids \cite{breuer}. The fact that the numerical setup succeeds in predicting this behavior confirms that the implemented bulk forcing, IBM cut-cell forcing, and the wall model are working correctly and are properly coupled with the Runge-Kutta scheme.

At the same time, the extension of the separation bubble presents a quantitative discrepancy with respect to the reference data. However, such a deviation is largely expected when considering that the specific flow dynamics, far from equilibrium conditions, were simulated with an equilibrium wall model. Furthermore, it is well-known that the reattachment point is extremely sensitive to the position of the flow separation point past the hill crest \cite{cinnella, breuer}, and as a consequence, also to the grid resolution, especially in the streamwise direction. At the considered Reynolds number, chosen at the maximum standard value to ensure reference data availability, this conflicts with the requirement of a sufficiently high $\Delta y^+$ to remain in the WM-LES domain. The particularly coarse grids adopted are the result of a trade-off between these aspects and the immersed geometry discretization.

\chapter{Complex geometry simulation and post-processing}
\label{sec:complex-geometry-simulation}
As previously mentioned, the primary advantage of the Immersed Boundary Method relies on the capability to simulate flows around complex geometries without the requirement of a body-conformal grid. However, ensuring numerical stability when dealing with highly irregular shapes is not trivial. The presence of sharp edges, high curvature regions, thin gaps and thin geometrical parts can cause several numerical stability issues, especially in the geometry forcing and interpolation modules. Therefore, to properly verify the robustness of the forcing method introduced in this work under particularly stressful conditions, some cases involving highly complex geometries were simulated. 

Instead of the benchmark geometries tested and quantitatively analized in the previous Chapter, in the following the focus is on a geometry representative of an industrial application. Such test cases allow to visually assess, in a qualitative way, the framework capability to correctly capture the main flow features and the expected aerodynamic phenomena associated with these configurations.

Furthermore, the implementation of the cut-cell forcing method provided the basis to develop a new post-processing approach, to represent the wall stress field directly on the original input geometry, allowing to visualize the wall stress distribution in a continuous surface rather than a discrete point cloud, with a better definition of the geometrical details.

\section{Wing-pylon-engine assembly}
The geometry adopted for the numerical simulations is representative of the wing\-/pylon\-/turbofan engine junction of an Airbus A320 commercial aircraft, isolated as a section from the model shown in Figure \ref{fig:A320-geometry-A} and \ref{fig:A320-geometry-B}. In particular, the region where the wing, the pylon, and the nacelle intersect is characterized by intricate geometrical features and narrow gaps. The turbofan engine is modeled in an inoperative condition and approximated in its internal parts: the bypass duct is unblocked, and the fan blades are represented in a stationary position, forming multiple flow passage regions through the inter-blade channels and allowing to capture the partial blockage effect characterizing the engine failure condition.

As previously introduced, the configuration was primarily chosen because it represents an example of a complex interface between multiple aerodynamic components, providing a severe test for the robustness of the IBM forcing and interpolation procedures. Furthermore, from an aerodynamic point of view, the multi-component assembly considered generates a highly complex three-dimensional flow field, characterized by interference phenomena.

\subsection{Simulation setup}
The computational domain was set with a streamwise length of $L_x = 15$, a height of $L_y = 7.5$, and a spanwise width of $L_z = 7.5$. The immersed geometry, consistently with the real configuration, is characterized by an engine front diameter $D = 2$, which is adopted as the reference length scale for the spatial discretization. In order to test the solver with different grid resolution, two simulation were performed:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \texttt{WPE-R1}: A coarser grid consisting of $400 \times 200 \times 200$ cells.
	\item \texttt{WPE-R2}: A refined grid consisting of $600 \times 300 \times 300$ cells.
\end{itemize} 
%
Regarding the boundary conditions, a uniform dimensionless freestream velocity profile $U_{\infty} = 1$ was prescribed at the domain inlet. At the outlet section, a homogeneous Neumann condition was applied. Finally, to simulate the undisturbed external flow surrounding the aircraft section, the lateral, top, and bottom boundaries were treated as far-field, enforcing the freestream velocity components.

In order to perform the test in a fully turbulent flow regime, representative of the physical phenomena involved, a Reynolds number $Re = 2 \times 10^6$ based on the engine diameter was selected, corresponding to a dimensionless kinematic viscosity of $\nu = 10^{-6}$.

The choice of the computational domain size, the grid resolution, the positioning and the scaling of the immersed geometry is the result of a trade-off between the quality of the component representation and the constraints of the available computational resources. Since the primary objective is to evaluate the IBM forcing on complex geometries, accurately resolving the solid boundaries was prioritized over the exact prediction of free-flight performances. Consequently, a solid blockage (or wind tunnel) effect was considered an acceptable compromise.

To enhance the numerical stability, two additional features have been implemented in the solver workflow, which can be enabled from the configuration input file: a velocity ramp-up function and a sponge layer function. The sponge layer, instead, acts at the very end of the streamwise domain, gradually dissipating the outgoing turbulent structures before they leave the computational grid. This approach proved effective in preventing spurious pressure reflections from moving upstream and disturbing the solution. Nevertheless, it is worth noting that these features demonstrated to be not strictly necessary for the solver stability, but were configured active anyway to ensure a cleaner and more physically consistent flow solution.

\subsection{Flow turbulent structures}
To qualitatively assess the solver capability to correctly capture the main aerodynamic features and the interference phenomena, the three-dimensional flow was post-processed with the $Q$-criterion vortex identification method.

Such criterion was one of those originally introduced by Hunt \textit{et al.} \cite{hunt}, to provide a generally agreed way to appropriately represent the recurring patterns characterizing the flow field, like eddies, convergence and streaming zones. Among these, eddy regions are of primary interest to properly visualize the flow topology and its turbulent structures. Since eddies are characterized as zones where the irrotational straining is small compared with the vorticity, the resulting mathematical formulation is:
\begin{equation}
	Q = \frac{1}{2} \left( \lVert \bm{\omega} \rVert^2 - \lVert \bm{e} \rVert^2 \right) > 0
    \label{eq:q-criterion}
\end{equation}
where $\lVert \bm{\omega} \rVert = \sqrt{\omega_{ij} \omega_{ij}}$ and $\lVert \bm{e} \rVert = \sqrt{e_{ij} e_{ij}}$ represent the Frobenius norms of the rotation rate tensor $\bm{\omega}$ and the strain rate tensor $\bm{e}$, respectively. The imposition of a $Q > 0$ condition, effectively filters out the flow regions dominated by pure localized shear, allowing to isolate and visualize the actual vortex cores.

To properly highlight the topology of the turbulent flow, a volume rendering technique was adopted for the $Q$ scalar field, using the open-source software ParaView. In particular, an opacity transfer function was tuned by visual inspection in the range $[5, 50]$, progressively increasing the opacity for higher values. This approach allows to gradually filter out the weaker turbulent structures. The velocity magnitude colorscale was then applied to the rendered volume, to provide a better understanding of the overall flow behavior. The resulting renderings for simulation \texttt{WPE-R2} are reported in Figures \ref{fig:A320-q-criterion-F}, \ref{fig:A320-q-criterion-L} and \ref{fig:A320-q-criterion-B}.

\subsection{Wall stress post-processing approach and distribution}
The implementation of the cut-cell forcing described in Section \ref{sec:cut-cell-forcing} has changed the definition of the interface cell. With this approach, a control volume is considered part of the numerical interface if it contains a non-null wetted area, or in other words a portion of the immersed geometry surface. Since the wall stress is computed in the cells that contain the geometrical interface, the adoption of this new definition provided the basis, firstly, to export a point cloud of the wall stress vectors at each time step; and secondly, to develop an accurate and computationally efficient post-processing technique to map any flow variable of interest directly onto the geometry surface, in its original STL format.

The idea of mapping any variable directly onto the input geometry has several advantages. Primarily, the original file is not affected by the grid discretization, in particular by its conversion into a Signed Distance Field (SDF) description. This means that the model geometry representation is decoupled from the grid resolution. Using the original STL as a base surface for the data, allows for a much more precise data localization and distribution, avoiding the geometric approximations introduced by the staircase representation. However, it is important to highlight that this method does not generate new data from nothing, but instead allows for its positioning on the high-quality geometrical model used as an input.

Typically, mapping flow variables onto the original surface would require an algorithm to project the point cloud data from the interface cells onto the model surface, and an interpolation step to obtain a distribution that uniformly covers the geometry. Such an approach would require a significant computational cost and implementation complexity, and would introduce some sort of modification of the solution data due to the interpolation method.

Conversely, with the method implemented in the present work, a simple re-intersection operation is sufficient. The process, represented in Figure \ref{fig:re-intersection}, consists of repositioning the original STL geometry within the computational grid and assigning to each portion of surface contained on the local interface cell, the flow variable defined in that cell, without the need of projection or interpolation methods.

\subsection{Result analysis}
The primary objective of the considered configuration was to provide a severe stress test for the stability of the numerical framework. The successful completion of both \texttt{WPE-R1} and \texttt{WPE-R2}, together with multiple preliminary test simulations with intricate geometries, highlighted the overall robustness of the solver. Specifically, throughout the entire time integration, the maximum divergence of the velocity field consistently remained below the prescribed safety threshold, in the order of $10^{-9}$.

% Upstream the engine, splitting
From the post-processing results, it is possible to provide a brief qualitative overview of the key flow features characterizing the numerical solution. Firstly, the incoming laminar flow is significantly deflected by the blockage induced by the inoperative engine, in a non-windmilling condition (no rotation), generating a high-pressure zone upstream the inlet cowl. Figures \ref{fig:A320-ingestion-streamlines} and \ref{fig:A320-streamlines} show the flow splitting into two portions: a smaller one ingested by the engine, while the vast majority passes externally. Furthermore, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:A320-wall-stress}, the stagnation points are clearly noticeable on the wing leading edge and on the nacelle lip ring.

% Ingested flow
The ingested flow undergoes a strong deceleration, a divergence of the streamlines, and a heavily reduced capture area. Reaching the fan disk, the streamlines, and consistently the wall stress distribution, show a sudden re-acceleration across the interblade channels. The fan blade row, operating in a severe off-design condition with massive flow misalignment, induces an extensive boundary layer separation, resulting in wide recirculation zones downstream of the fan. Downstream of the bypass duct, a slight re-acceleration is observed across the bypass nozzle, before the flow mixes again with the external stream.

% External flow
Due to the severe engine obstruction, a large portion of the freestream approaching the engine inlet is abruptly deflected radially outwards, accelerating over the nacelle lip, in a inlet spillage condition. Downstream of the lip region, the flow decelerates to recover the external freestream conditions, undergoing an adverse pressure gradient. This behavior is consistent with what is observed in terms of wall shear stress magnitude.

% Wake
In terms of wake formation, the flow generates distinct regions downstream of the engine (mixing with the nozzle exit flow), the flap track fairings, and the wing trailing edge. As depicted in Figures \ref{fig:A320-q-criterion-L} and \ref{fig:A320-q-criterion-B}, it is possible to observe that the turbulent structures populating the wake grow and develop during the startup transient.

% Limitations/separation
Finally, across various simulations, some limitations of the IBM framework were noticed. When modeling surfaces that should be perfectly smooth in reality, a tendency to anticipate boundary layer separation is observed. This behavior is likely due to the numerical roughness introduced by the grid discretization. This hypothesis is supported by the fact that the phenomenon appears to be significantly amplified by coarser grid resolutions.

\chapter{Conclusions}
This thesis detailed the development, integration, testing, and application of a refined immersed boundary forcing approach, based on the analytical computation of the cut-cell wetted area and coupled with a flux redistribution method. The evaluation of the wetted area for each grid cell allowed a reformulation of the numerical interface definition. The initial approach identified the interface through sign changes of the level set function across adjacent cell nodes, resulting in a staircase wall approximation. In contrast, the proposed method defines interface cells as those effectively containing a non-zero wetted area, applying the wall-model forcing term directly to this analytically evaluated surface. This geometric characterization enables a more uniform and consistent application of the immersed boundary forcing term. The algorithm for the wetted area computation for each control volume of the domain was designed, integrated and optimized such that no significant increments on the computational cost were noticed.

The proposed formulation was tested against two benchmarks. First, it was applied to the canonical turbulent channel flow at a Reynolds number $Re = 80000$. The solver accurately predicted the mean flow behavior, demonstrating a good agreement with the analytical Reichardt law. Secondly, the solver was tested against the ERCOFTAC periodic hills benchmark at $Re = 37000$. This case presents a significantly more complex flow topology, characterized by flow separation from a curved boundary, a large recirculation bubble, and flow reattachment. The simulations, performed on two different grids, demonstrated the solver's capability to capture the macroscopic features of the flow, including the acceleration over the crest, the formation of the free shear layer, and the flow reattachment. While quantitative deviations in the reattachment point location were observed compared to WR-LES and experimental reference data, these discrepancies were largely expected, considering the overall grid resolution requirements and the use of an equilibrium wall model.

Finally, to assess the stability of the numerical framework under demanding conditions, the solver was applied to simulate a highly complex geometry representative of an Airbus A320 wing-pylon-engine assembly. This configuration, featuring an inoperative (non windmilling) turbofan engine, intricate junctions, narrow gaps, and multiple flow passages, provided a severe stress test for the robustness of the IBM forcing and interpolation procedures. The successful completion of the simulations, maintaining the velocity divergence well below the safety threshold throughout the time integration, demonstrated the numerical framework's capability to handle such cases without stability issues. Qualitative flow visualization, achieved through the $Q$-criterion and streamline analysis, indicated that the solver is capable of reproducing several macroscopic aerodynamic features consistent with the expected physical behavior, including the strong flow deflection upstream of the partially blocked nacelle, the inlet spillage, the flow acceleration through the stationary fan blades, and the complex wake development.

Despite the overall robustness and the advantages offered by the IBM approach, the analysis also highlighted an intrinsic limitation of the current framework. Specifically, when modeling surfaces that should be perfectly smooth in reality, the solver exhibits a tendency to anticipate boundary layer separation. This behavior is likely attributable to the numerical roughness introduced by the grid discretization of the curved boundaries. This hypothesis is further supported by the observation that the premature separation phenomenon is significantly amplified when coarser grid resolutions are employed, indicating a strong sensitivity of the near-wall flow dynamics to the geometric representation.

The re-definition of the numerical interface obtained implementing the wetted area computation introduced in this work, provided the numerical basis for a novel post-processing methodology. It became possible to map, with a simple re-intersection operation, any physical variable (such as pressure, wall shear stress, ...) directly onto the continuous surface of the original STL geometry. This method eliminates the need for complex data projection or interpolation algorithms. The approach, tested with the wall-stress data, produced significantly higher-fidelity renderings, overcoming the visual limitations of discrete point clouds.
